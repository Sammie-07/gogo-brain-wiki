# Mindset

[[Gogo Bethke]]'s foundational belief system — a mix of law of attraction, radical personal accountability, and the discipline to stay in your lane. She treats business success as primarily a spiritual and mental game.

> "Business is a spiritual game. Everything that you have, by far, are the thoughts in your head."

## The Law of Attraction

Gogo is an explicit believer in *The Secret* / law of attraction. Core mechanic: the universe takes your thoughts as frequencies that are already true. What you believe you can have, you attract. What you believe you can't have, you repel.

> "If you believe it's possible, the universe literally has to make it happen. It does not have a choice."

The *how* is not your job. Your only job is to believe the goal is already yours. The universe handles the path.

## "If I Cannot Imagine It, I Cannot Have It"

The single most important mindset rule Gogo teaches. If you write down a goal but internally feel "there's no way," that feeling blocks it.

The fix: **meditation/visualization practice.**
- Sit quietly until you can vividly picture yourself at the goal
- Close your eyes; see the specific scene — the stage, the condo, the car, the cash
- Do not get up until the "there's no way" twitch is gone
- Gogo treats this as mandatory before any new goal is real

> "I put myself in a corner and I think about what I just did to myself. How I self-sabotaged my own dreams. And I sit there until I can see myself on that level doing that thing."

## No Plan B

Having a fallback isn't humility — it's a belief that the primary goal isn't achievable. That belief undermines the law of attraction and confuses the internal GPS.

See [[Goal Setting]] for the GPS metaphor in full.

## "What I Don't Change, I Accept"

Personal accountability formula. Wherever you are right now is the standard you set for yourself and allowed. Not a judgment — a fact. The moment you see it clearly, you can change it.

> "If you don't like your results, fine. Own up to it and let's change it."

This is why Gogo opens goal-setting sessions by having people write down their current reality honestly — you can't navigate from a false starting point.

## You Are the Average of the Five People Around You

Gogo applies this principle across all domains:
- Brokerage choice: be where high-producing agents are (see [[Real Estate Math]] / PPP)
- Masterminds and events: always seek rooms where you're not the smartest
- Personal network: audit the income of your five closest people — you're likely close to their average

> "As soon as you feel like the smartest person in the room, you're in the wrong room."

## Continuous Learning as Identity

Gogo describes herself as someone who wants to know enough to be dangerous in everything she's interested in. She attends seminars, reads constantly, goes to masterminds, and pushes for Q&A time when sessions open up. Learning isn't a task — it's her identity.

> "I want to know enough to be dangerous in just about everything that I'm interested in."

Rule: the moment you feel you've mastered a room, it's time to move to the next level.

## Celebrate the Wins

Dangling carrots are a legitimate motivational tool. Gogo sets personal rewards (a Gucci belt, a spa day, a trip) and works toward them. The psychological mechanism: knowing you've *earned* a reward makes you work harder and feel better about the journey.

Celebrating wins builds momentum. Each achieved goal proves the system works, which strengthens belief for the next one.

## The CEO Mindset Shift

One of Gogo's signature frameworks: the moment you stop seeing yourself as a realtor and start seeing yourself as the CEO of a business, everything changes.

**Agent mindset:** chase the next transaction → income disappears when you stop showing up → no trackers → no tax strategy → no investments → no life.

**CEO / entrepreneur mindset:** build systems and structures → create passive and recurring income → know your hourly rate → hire everything below that rate → your money makes money while you sleep.

> "Stop thinking of yourself as just a worker in your business. Think of yourself as the CEO of your business. People will take your business seriously when you take your business seriously."

**You become a different person to make different money.** Gogo made $16,000 in year one (2011). The person who made $16,000 could not have made millions — that person had to evolve. Growth requires becoming someone new.

> "The person who made $50,000 could have not made millions. That means I had to become a different person with a different mindset."

## You Can Have It All

A common limiting belief — installed in childhood — is that you must choose between things you want. "Do you want chips or ice cream? Pick one." This embeds scarcity as a default frame.

Gogo's reframe: you don't have to choose. Career and freedom. Empire and family. Travel and roots. When you catch yourself saying "I have to choose between X and Y," replace it with: **"Why do I have to choose? Why can't I have both?"**

> "You can have it all. We can all have it all. The problem is we are all trained that we have to choose between this or that."

The retrained question: *Why can't I have it all?*

## "We All Live in Cages With the Doors Wide Open"

George Lucas quote that Gogo says "literally changed my life." Something in your childhood or life made you accept a limitation — a belief that you were too young, too dumb, too uneducated, too late. That moment is when you accepted the cage.

The door is wide open. You can walk out at any time.

> "We all live in cages with the doors wide open. The question is: who put you there?"

## Break the Rules, Not the Law

> "It's better to ask for forgiveness than for permission."

Rules were created by someone who could only see one possibility. Gogo pushes rules constantly ("you can ask my team how many times I push it") — while maintaining zero parking tickets. Never break the law; question every rule.

## Faith Over Fear

Fear only creeps in when faith is missing. When you feel afraid, the antidote isn't suppression — it's increasing your faith (in yourself, your goal, the universe). Listen to your gut.

## Don't Ask Broke Friends for Business Advice

Separate personal relationships from business counsel. You can love someone who's broke — just don't discuss business ideas with them or ask their opinion on your goals. They'll tell you it's impossible because they literally cannot see the possibility.

> "Never ask a broke friend how to make a million dollars. They will tell you it's impossible because they don't know how."

## Belief vs. Value (Gary Coxe)

You can *believe* you deserve success without actually *valuing* it enough to act on it consistently. Behavior follows values, not beliefs. Most mindset work focuses on belief reprogramming — the real lever is subconscious values.

> The gap between where you are and where you want to be is often not a belief problem — it's a values problem.

Feelings (fear, doubt, unworthiness) override rational belief in the moment. Identify what you actually *value* at a subconscious level, and reprogram that.

## Identity Reprogramming (Rock Thomas)

Behavior change is short-lived unless rooted in identity. "I am a person who…" statements shape what actions feel natural vs. forced.

**The formula:** Repetition + emotion = new neural pathway. Affirmations without emotional charge don't work. Repeated belief + felt experience rewires the subconscious.

**"I am" statements:** What follows "I am" is your most powerful identity declaration. *I am a millionaire. I am a leader.* Not "I want to be."

Environmental design matters: proximity to high-performers accelerates identity reprogramming.

## Unrealistic Goals Are the Only Goals Worth Setting (Verl Workman)

Goals you can easily achieve don't require growth. Only goals that feel out of reach force the identity and behavior changes that produce real results. Don't manage the outcome — manage the daily activity that leads to it.

Both Gogo and Verl Workman discuss **breaking the poverty cycle** — the mindset inherited from families without wealth that each generation must consciously reprogram.

## Money Frequency

Your relationship with money is a frequency you can tune. People who grew up without money often carry a scarcity frequency that repels wealth even when income rises. Mindset shift alone isn't enough — it must be combined with financial literacy (tracking, advisory team, P&L). Mindset without mechanics = money leaks. Mechanics without mindset = self-sabotage.

## "The Most Costly Thing in Life Is a Closed Mind"

> "The most interesting thing that's going to cost you the most in this life is a closed mind. If you're not open to new ideas, if you're not open to new opportunities, they are just not going to show up."

Repeated across multiple sources as one of Gogo's signature principles. Being open to eXp, to AI, to new business models — all came from refusing to close her mind to the unfamiliar.

## Adaptability as Survival (Aaron Novello)

> "The most adaptable species survive. Darwin was talking about Realtors and he didn't even know it."

In market downturns, the agents who thrive are not the strongest or most experienced — they're the most adaptable. Novello's "Season of Disappointment" frame: the current market isn't a crash, it's a prolonged adjustment. Hope is not a strategy. Plan for the market as it is.

**The addiction problem:** Most agents are addicted not to money but to *how* they receive it (the traditional buyer/seller model). Market shifts force diversification.

## The 5-Years Rule (Beth Silverman)

> "Five years. Commit to five years and you will not recognize yourself."

Full commitment to something for five years produces transformation that's impossible to achieve in shorter cycles. Most people quit before year 5 — which is exactly when the compounding payoff arrives.

**"Compress time to achieve outcome":** Don't pace yourself across three years when intensity can achieve the same result in eight months. Temporary sacrifice is rational when the outcome is permanent.

**"Can't deposit opinions in a bank account":** Ignore critics. Revenue is the only scoreboard that matters. What others think about your methods is irrelevant; what shows up in your bank account is not.

## No-Show Policy (Beth Silverman)

One no-show from a client or appointment = excusable. Second no-show → blocked forever. Non-negotiable. Time is too valuable to protect anyone who doesn't respect yours.

## Manifestation Identity (Kathleen Cameron)

Manifestation begins with identity, not action. You must *own* the identity of what you want before it materializes — not want it, not work toward it, but accept it as already true.

**Kathleen Cameron's framework:**
- Identify what you want to manifest
- Adopt the identity of that version of yourself now
- Align energy/vibration through both mindset (practical) and spirituality (connection to something larger than yourself)
- The imagination is the primary tool — what you can vividly hold in imagination, you can experience

Kathleen's own journey began after meeting Bob Proctor in 2019. Related to Gogo's law of attraction practice (see above).

## Drag Metaphor — Cut the Baggage (Gary Coxe)

Aviation metaphor: **drag** is the force that slows an aircraft. Some drag is necessary — the same physics that create drag also create lift. But unnecessary drag (emotional baggage, toxic relationships, self-doubt, distractions) slows you without providing any lift.

**Framework:**
1. Identify what's slowing you down
2. Evaluate: is it necessary drag (helps you fly) or unnecessary drag (pure weight)?
3. Release the unnecessary
4. Result: you don't just move forward — you take off

Gary Coxe specializes in producing this shift in one session. See also [[Control Your Emotions Control Your Income (Gary Coxe)]].

## Communication Styles — Listening vs. Solving (Gogo's Marriage Insight)

Even high-performers have communication mismatches. Gogo and Dwayne: both high-D (Dominant) personalities — both fixers who instinctively want to solve. This creates friction when one person needs to be heard rather than fixed.

**Solution — hand signals:**
- Ear signal = "I just need you to listen."
- Mouth signal = "I want your solution."
Removes ambiguity. Prevents the most common conflict between problem-solvers.

Alex Hormozi's insight: women generally talk to process and be heard; men generally want to provide solutions. Knowing this prevents it from becoming personal.

## "Their Opinion Doesn't Pay Your Bills"

> "I start caring about your opinion as soon as I can deposit it into my bank account."

Critics will always exist. They already have an opinion of you before you've posted a single thing. Trying to prevent judgment is wasted energy — they're going to judge either way.

The reframe: **you should start worrying when people stop talking about you**, because that means you became irrelevant. Criticism is proof you showed up.

**The people filter:** "I'm looking for the people who are looking for me." (Credit: Brandon Barnum.) Create content, show up, build a brand — not for the critics, but for the specific people who will resonate with who you are. The others are simply not your people. They will never be your clients regardless of what you do.

**The business closer:** Their opinion doesn't allow you to retire. It doesn't send your kid to college. But when they hire you as their realtor, it does. Create content so the right people can choose you.

This principle governs Gogo's willingness to show up on camera without makeup, with imperfect lighting, with her stomach showing — because the alternative (not showing up) costs her far more than any critic's opinion ever could.

## "I Found Something Fascinating" (Bad News Protocol)

When delivering bad news to clients, open with: **"I found something fascinating…"** instead of leading with the problem. Curiosity replaces alarm. Keeps the conversation productive before the emotional reaction sets in.

Independently developed by Gogo; validated by Chris Voss (FBI negotiation) on the Gogopreneur podcast. See [[Negotiation]].

## "Know Who Knows" (Capacity Framework)

> "Our brain capacity is limited. We don't need to know everything. We just need to know the person who knows."

Knowing everything is not the goal. Knowing who to call for each domain is a higher-leverage skill. Gogo has never personally logged into most of the tools her business runs on (Zapier, ManyChat, GHL, Skye). She knows what they do and what she needs; her team handles the how. This is not a gap -- it is intentional design.

## Natural Sleep Schedule Philosophy

You do not need to be an early riser to be successful. Gogo wakes at 8:30am and goes to bed around 1am. Tony Robbins goes to bed at 3:40am and wakes at 9am. Forcing an unnatural schedule fights your biology and depletes the energy you need for high-performance work.

> "I follow my natural sleeping schedule. I go to bed when I get tired and I wake up when I'm done sleeping."

The relevant variable is not when you sleep -- it is what you do with your awake hours, and whether those hours are protected by systems and a team.

## Money Mindset (Barbara Schreihans)

Barbara Schreihans -- founder of Your Tax Coach, Inc 5000 #513 -- opened her GoGet'Em Community "marathon of the soul" session with a raw personal origin story (born addicted to crack cocaine, abusive and addicted parents, started working at 12, graduated high school at 15, enormous debt in early adulthood) before delivering her money mindset framework.

**Your relationship with money is inherited**
Your money relationship is a blend of your mother's relationship with money and your father's relationship with money -- even if you consciously reject their patterns, the unconscious wiring is there.

**The journaling exercise:**
1. Write your mother's relationship with money (was money a secret? Did she work multiple jobs? Did she talk about it or hide it?)
2. Write your father's relationship with money (did he hand over his paycheck and receive an allowance? Did he use layaway or payday loans?)
3. Compare both. Find yourself in both patterns.

Common inherited patterns: money as a topic never discussed openly, one partner controlled all finances, spending triggered guilt or shame, payday loans and layaway normalized as financial tools. Historical context: women could not hold credit cards in their own name until roughly 50 years ago.

> "No matter what your story is, no matter what your mess is, it can be your message."

**Celebrating money as a mindset practice**
Barbara noticed her income increased when she returned to intentionally celebrating wins after a period of not doing so. This is not indulgence -- it is feedback to your subconscious that money is welcome.

- Little wins: afford a house cleaner ($150/week) -- celebrate it
- Medium wins: spa day or champagne when hitting a monthly business goal
- Big wins: designer bag every time she closes on an investment property

Celebrate every dollar coming in AND every dollar going out -- gratitude for the ability to pay bills. Pick up every penny off the ground. Say thank you. "If you scoff at the little stuff, the universe thinks you're not ready for the big stuff."

Book recommendation: *Happy Money* -- teaches celebrating every dollar that leaves your account.

**No morning routine -- and that's fine**
Barbara explicitly does not have a 4am wake-up routine. She builds a life where work doesn't feel like work and life isn't hindered by work. Not every high-performer needs the same morning architecture. What matters is what you do with your awake hours, not when they start.

> "You don't have to follow the 4am wake-up call to be super successful. Some of my things that don't feel authentic to me -- I don't have a morning routine."

**Purpose as anchor**
Barbara's empire exists because of one thing: if saving money helps even one woman escape an abusive relationship, the effort is worth it. One in six women are in abusive relationships. Purpose that extends beyond personal enrichment is what sustains the long-term build.

> "If I could help save money for one woman that could get out of a shitty abusive relationship, that was my purpose."

## Energetic Set Point and Vessel Expansion (Craig Siegel)

Identity — not effort, not tactics, not market conditions — determines your ceiling. Each person has an invisible energetic set point that caps their results. Working harder inside a fixed set point produces diminishing returns; you hit a ceiling and wonder why effort isn't translating.

**The vessel metaphor (Kabbalah):** You cannot pour more water into a vessel that hasn't expanded. Before more can arrive — clients, income, opportunities — you must enlarge your capacity to receive. The vessel expansion *precedes* the result.

**Quantum leaps:** Transformation is not always linear. When identity shifts at a deep enough level, results can jump dramatically without proportional increase in effort. The "overnight success" is usually a sudden identity shift that had been building underneath.

**Identity over strategy.** The strategy is rarely the problem. The person executing it is. Two agents with identical strategies produce radically different results because of who they are while executing.

> "Stop trying to change your actions. Start changing who you are." — Craig Siegel's core teaching

## Nervous System as Leadership Foundation (Ludmila Woodruff)

Most leadership breakdowns aren't strategy failures — they're nervous system failures. Leaders who cannot regulate their own emotional state under pressure cannot create psychological safety for their teams.

**The three-pillar sequence:**
1. **Nervous system regulation first.** Stress and conflict activate survival responses (fight, flight, freeze). Leaders who learn to recognize and interrupt these responses operate from clarity rather than reaction. You cannot think strategically from a dysregulated state.
2. **Emotional mastery.** Not suppression — intelligent awareness and direction of emotion. What you feel in a meeting shows up in how people receive your words, energy, and presence, even when you say nothing.
3. **Real-time tools.** In-the-moment practices for staying grounded during high-stakes conversations: difficult feedback, conflict, disappointment, accountability conversations.

**Presence = the prerequisite.** Being present (not reactive) is not optional for leaders — it is the foundation skill everything else rests on.

## Conscious Communication (Jamie Miller)

Most communication is autopilot — default patterns learned in childhood, triggered under pressure. Conscious communication is the deliberate practice of choosing words, tone, and presence rather than falling into inherited patterns.

**Words create reality:** "I *have to* prospect" vs. "I *get to* connect with people today" — same action, completely different emotional reality and energy brought to execution. The language you use shapes how the work feels and how effectively you do it.

**Self-talk under stress** defaults to the least conscious (most critical, most fearful) patterns. The inner voice narrating your experience is also subject to conscious vs. unconscious communication.

**For real estate leaders:** How you speak to your team determines team performance more than any system or incentive. Unconscious leadership creates confusion, fear, and underperformance. Psychological safety is a product of conscious, consistent leadership language.

## Season of Disappointment (Aaron Novello)

2024 was statistically one of the worst years for real estate transactions in 15 years — fewer closings than 2008. Most agents treated this temporary season as a permanent condition and made permanent decisions (quit, pivot, stop prospecting) at precisely the wrong moment.

**Disappointment is data, not a verdict.** The hardest seasons contain lessons that winning seasons cannot teach. What you do during invisible results — when activity doesn't immediately produce outcomes — is what determines where you are when conditions improve.

**Resilience framework:** Don't quit. Don't pivot without cause. Stay visible. Keep prospecting. Serve the community.

## Big Obnoxious Goals (Gogo Bethke)

"If your goals are not scaring you shitless, you didn't set big obnoxious goals. You settled."

Most people set goals they know they can hit — protecting their ego at the expense of their results. They lower the bar so they look and feel better when they reach it. The result: predictable mediocrity.

The alternative: set a goal that makes you shiver. Then reverse-engineer it into daily bites. The scariness is not a warning to stop — it's a signal that the goal is big enough to actually change your life.

## Hardship as Unlearned Lesson (Gogo Bethke)

A recurring hardship is not bad luck — it is a lesson you haven't yet learned. The hardship returns in different forms until the lesson is integrated. This frames adversity as instructive rather than punitive: the question is not "why is this happening to me?" but "what am I being shown that I'm not seeing?"

Applied practically: before making a major change to escape a difficult situation, ask whether the difficulty is signaling something that needs to change internally, not just externally.

## Background Is Not Destiny (Krista Mashore)

[[Krista Mashore]] — 2,300+ homes sold, $45M in online course revenue, mastermind partner with Gogo — opened her GoGet'Em session by rejecting the idea that a difficult past limits your future.

**Her origin:** Childhood abuse, extreme poverty, put herself through college and started in real estate with nothing. No safety net. No support system. No favorable circumstances.

**The core message:** Every limitation you cite — your upbringing, your market, your team, your lack of resources — is a story you've chosen to keep telling. She stopped telling hers. What changed wasn't her circumstances; it was her decision about what those circumstances meant.

**Applied to real estate:** You do not need a great market, a great broker, a great team, or a great list to build a great business. You need a decision, a system, and the willingness to run the same play long enough for it to work.

> "I came from nothing and look what's possible. What's your excuse?"

**Her content framework (relevant to mindset):** She made over 2,000 videos in her career — many uncomfortable, many imperfect. The commitment to showing up consistently, even when the result isn't polished, is itself a mindset position. Most agents wait until they're "ready." Ready doesn't exist until after you've started.

## Immigrant Deserving Mindset — "Why Aren't They Here?" (Alex Moeller)

[[Alex Moeller]] and Gogo both reflect the immigrant experience of self-sabotage rooted in a sense of not deserving more than family members who didn't emigrate. Alex's framing:

> "I still had this feeling of like, why would I deserve more? I have a hard work ethic. So do my parents. So does my sister. So why would I deserve more than anybody else in my family? And then one time I was at a bonfire with some random person and he goes: 'So if you're comparing yourself to your family and your sister, then why aren't they here?' Oh. Because I did that."

The reframe: **you deserve what you built.** If others wanted it, they would have done what you did. No one handed it to you. The decision to cross a continent, enter an industry you didn't know, and build from zero is itself the justification.

Gogo's parallel: raised in Eastern Europe, spent years unlearning a mindset that blocked American-scale success.

> "I came for the American dream and I cannot build an American dream on an Eastern European mindset foundation. Not gonna work." — Gogo

This connects to the broader "why aren't they here?" reframe: wherever you are financially is the standard you set and allowed. And wherever you want to be is available to you — if you build differently.

## The Three States (Louise — GoGet'Em Guest)

Faith-based business coach Louise presented a framework on the three states entrepreneurs cycle through — and how to choose the higher one intentionally.

**Survival State** — Fear, anxiety, depression. Often triggered by loss, financial pressure, or uncertainty. People in this state make reactive decisions and unconsciously block opportunity.

**Transition State** — Uncertainty and stress while building something new. The "incubation/cocoon state" — deep internal work is happening even when external results aren't visible. The lesson: understanding the season helps you endure it. *"You can be certain of uncertainty."*

**Higher State (Flow)** — Wisdom, creativity, flow. The state where best work happens. For Louise: connected to God and worship. For others: nature, swimming, deep creative work. The point: identify what takes you there and choose it intentionally — especially when you least feel like it.

Key insight: entrepreneurs often access higher state in their passion areas but drop to survival state around selling or money. Reframing selling as "an exchange of gold for gold" reconnects worthiness to the act.

## Be Still and Know — Prophetic Intelligence (Louise)

"Everything is permissible, but not everything is profitable." — The filter creative, high-output people need. The brain produces ~1,400 words/minute. Creatives may experience triple that. Learning to slow the brain is a prerequisite to accessing higher state.

**"Be still and know"** — A learnable skill set that no motivational event teaches in isolation. You have a knowing inside you. Access it through stillness.

**Neuroscience evidence (Andrew Newberg):** Studies on nuns and monks in prayer showed loss of time and space — mapped on SPECT brain scans. The brain enters a state of higher consciousness that intersects with spiritual intelligence. Goal: access this state as a regular business tool, not just a spiritual one. The result: "prophetic strategy and maps and blueprints" that answer questions faster than tactical thinking.

The higher-state marker: loss of time and space. Working with quiet intensity, not frantic effort. Louise's metaphor — "like a whale under the water, strongly and elegantly going through the water."

## Pioneer Mindset and Assignment (Louise)

**Your assignment** = what you've labored through, the trauma you haven't given up on. In that place, you have authority. You've birthed something for other people.

Entrepreneurial mindset = pioneer mindset. Employee mindset cannot coexist with successful entrepreneurship. Pioneers go first — with no one beside them. Like David training in hiding before facing Goliath.

**Alignment** = coming out of agreement with doubt, shame, and fear. Everyone feels these — the variable is how much attention you give them. Letting them keep you in hiding when the world is waiting for you is the cost.

**Execution as a skill** — Many creatives stay stuck in ideas, watering too many fields. Learning to execute with joy, to take an idea all the way through, is a learnable competency — not just a personality trait.

When emotional patterns are misaligned → financial frustration, brand stagnation, no traction. Alignment precedes results.
