# Personal Branding

[[Gogo Bethke]]'s framework for building, centralizing, and tracking your personal brand — starting with a complete audit of where you are, then measuring growth every month without fail.

> "The only reason you should ever look back is to see how far you've come."

## The Brand Tracker Document

A single master sheet containing every brand asset. Gogo requires this document from members of her **Audit Club** and recommends every agent maintain one at all times.

**What goes in it:**

| Field | Example (Gogo's) |
|-------|-----------------|
| Name | Gogo Bethke |
| Brand / username | Gogo's Real Estate |
| Instagram profile link | direct URL |
| Facebook link | direct URL |
| YouTube link | direct URL |
| LinkedIn link | direct URL |
| TikTok link | (if applicable) |
| Personal website | gogobethke.com |
| Real estate website | gogorealestateteam.com (kvCORE) |
| Google Business Profile | direct URL |
| Branded email | christy@gogorealestate.com (example format) |
| Headshot links | cloud folder or direct URLs |
| Bio link | direct URL |
| Smart phone number | kvCORE number |
| Brand hashtags | #gogobethke, #gogorealestate, #gogetemcommunity |

**Why it matters:** At any moment — for a coaching call, an audit, a collaboration, or a rebrand — everything needed to represent your brand is in one place. No hunting across platforms.

## The Growth Tracker

A monthly habit built into the Brand Tracker document. Habit stack: set a recurring phone reminder for the **2nd of every month**.

**What to log each month:**
- Follower count per platform
- Average monthly views per platform

**The purpose:** Social media growth is slow and invisible day-to-day. Monthly snapshots accumulate into proof. By December, comparing January to December shows the compounding effect of consistent effort — and creates social proof ("I went from 5,678 to 23,000 followers this year").

This mirrors Gogo's broader [[Productivity]] philosophy: track everything so you make data-driven decisions and can see the truth of your progress.

## Own Your Name

> "Your domain is literally just like your house's address — it's going to be the address for your website."

Owning your domain is the foundation of your brand. The domain is just the address — you'll link it to a website, funnel, or landing page later. The goal right now is to **lock in the name before someone else does.**

**Two domains to buy:**
1. **yourname.com** — personal brand
2. **yourbusinessname.com** — business brand (e.g., gogorealestateteam.com)

Gogo's: gogobethke.com (personal) + gogorealestateteam.com (real estate business).

### Domain Forwarding to Your BoldTrail/KVCore Website

eXp agents receive a default BoldTrail website URL (e.g., `firstname-demo.exprealty.com`) — unusable for marketing. **Domain forwarding** redirects your clean custom domain to it for free (you've already paid for the domain).

**Steps (GoDaddy):**
1. Copy your BoldTrail/KVCore website URL
2. GoDaddy → 9-dot menu → My Account → **Domains** → find your domain → three dots → **Forward**
3. Paste your BoldTrail URL → preview to verify
4. Choose **Forwarding with Masking** — keeps your custom domain in the visitor's address bar; they never see the ugly eXp URL. Allows meta keywords for SEO.
5. Add meta keywords (e.g., "homes for sale," your city name) → Save

Works identically on Namecheap, Google Domains, Squarespace — every registrar supports forwarding.

> "You just copy the link of the ugly website and paste it into the domain forwarding on whatever platform hosts that domain."

### GoDaddy Purchase Walkthrough
*(as demonstrated by [[Kristy Waker]] in GSMC 2026)*

1. Go to **GoDaddy** → search your exact brand name (should match your Instagram handle already verified)
2. Confirm it's available — first-year pricing is often discounted (~$4.99)
3. **Enable auto-renew** (annually) — if you let it lapse, someone else can buy it out from under your live website
4. **Skip all add-ons**: domain privacy, protections, extras — say no to everything
5. **Do NOT buy the GoDaddy/Microsoft email** — real estate runs on Google; set up professional email through Google Workspace separately later
6. Complete purchase → copy domain URL → paste into Brand Tracker (website field) → check it off

You don't need to build the website yet. Just own the address.

## Your Feed Is Your Storefront

*Source: [[Remove Old, Confusing Branding (Instagram Feed Audit)]]*

> "Think of it when someone lands on your Instagram as your storefront. If I had a physical store and I was selling real estate, but my storefront window had a mannequin in yoga outfits, sushi, a cat, a dog, three kids, and pictures from Italy, what would you think I'm selling?"

Your Instagram (and Facebook) **feed is your business storefront** — permanent, public, and the first thing any stranger sees. It must communicate one thing without ambiguity: real estate.

**What does NOT belong in your feed:**
- Kids, pets, food, travel, sports, yoga, hobbies — unless the post itself is about a real estate moment that happened in that context
- Maximum **2 holiday posts per year** in the feed (e.g., Christmas, Mother's Day)
- Anything that would confuse a first-time visitor about what you sell

**What IS allowed:**
- Personal aesthetics (yoga pants, lifestyle imagery) if what you're *talking about* is real estate
- Kids/pets if the post subject is a real estate moment — *"It's not about little Johnny winning a game; it's about having a closing while sitting at the game"*

**Stories vs. Feed:**
- Feed = permanent storefront; keep it professional and real-estate-focused
- Stories = 24-hour disappearing content; safe space for behind-the-scenes, personal moments, lifestyle

**How to clean up an existing account:**
- Do NOT start a new account — rebuilding an audience from zero is too costly
- Go through your feed and delete every post unrelated to real estate
- Move personal content to Stories going forward (or a private personal account)

**Privacy argument:** At millions of views, exposing your children to strangers becomes a genuine risk. Keeping them off public feeds protects them regardless of business logic.

> "Your feed is your storefront. Your stories appear for 24 hours and then disappear."

## Gogo's Brand Evolution

Her brand grew through distinct phases, each with its own identity:

1. **Gogo's Real Estate** — original brand, still her primary username across platforms
2. **Gogo Bethke** — personal brand as her name became the brand
3. **Gogopreneur** — entrepreneurship-focused brand extension
4. **[[GoGet'Em Community]]** — community brand for her broader audience

The lesson: a personal brand can spawn multiple sub-brands over time. Start with one; let the others emerge naturally as your reach expands.

## Same Username Everywhere

> "Every time you come up with a new idea — a new coaching program, a digital product, a speaking engagement, a podcast — that's the first thing you do. GoDaddy and Instagram."

Companion principle to Same Photo Everywhere. Name consistency = brand recognition. McDonald's is McDonald's everywhere — name, logo, everything. Your username must work the same way.

### Two Platforms You Must Own (Non-Negotiable)

1. **yourname.com** — only one .com with that exact name exists. Buy it on GoDaddy.
2. **Your Instagram handle** — only one account with that exact username exists.

Facebook and LinkedIn are lower priority — duplicate names can coexist there. But .com and Instagram are unique. Own them now, even if you're not ready to build — once someone else buys them, you'll have to pay to get them back (or you can't).

### How to Check Availability

1. Go to **GoDaddy** → search the .com
2. Go to **Instagram** → search the handle directly
   - Namechk (namechk.com) exists for multi-platform checks but Instagram direct search is more reliable

### Once You Own It

- Set **auto-renew on GoDaddy** (~$11/year). If you lapse, someone can buy your domain right out from under your live website.
- Use the **same username everywhere**: don't be "The Luxe Group" on Instagram and "BrightonsTopProducingRealtor.com" on your website. People can't connect the dots.

### Cross-Linking All Platforms

Once every profile is set up, do a **second pass** to connect them all:
- Instagram bio → links to website + Facebook
- Facebook page → links to Instagram + website
- YouTube channel → links to Instagram + Facebook + website
- Website → links to all social platforms
- Google Business → links to website

Every platform is an entry point. A stranger who finds you anywhere must be able to reach your full brand ecosystem from that single touchpoint. Same cover photo + same headshot + same username = instant recognition across every platform.

### New Brand / Venture Checklist

Every time you start something new — course, coaching program, podcast, speaking brand, local real estate team:
1. Check .com availability on GoDaddy
2. Check Instagram handle availability
3. If both are available → buy them immediately
4. Then take a breath and build at whatever pace you need

### Pro Tip: Buy Your Children's Names

Buy yourchild'sname.com now. ~$11/year. You don't know which one becomes the next Elon Musk. Own the asset; they can build on it later.

**Gogo's story:** She bought her son Koby's domain ~14 years before he'd ever use it. On his 18th birthday, she gifted it to him — a web address already aged, already his. Cost: ~$150 total over 14 years.

### Never Include Your Location in Your Brand Name

> "Never include your location in your brand name."

Gogo spent 14 years building the brand "Gogo's Real Estate" and "Gogo's Michigan Real Estate" — then moved from Michigan to Florida. Every piece of Michigan-specific branding became a liability overnight: signs, cards, websites, social handles, SEO.

**The rule:** Your brand name must travel with you anywhere. A location locks you to a geography you may leave. A personal name (Gogo Bethke) or generic business name goes wherever you go.

This is one of Gogo's most-cited personal lessons — held up as the expensive mistake she never wants her community to repeat.

### Gogo's Brand Evolution (Context)

She started everything as *Gogo's Real Estate* — built around transactions and commissions. As she grew into speaking, podcasts, bootcamps, and the [[GoGet'Em Community]], that brand no longer fit. She is now migrating everything to *Gogo Bethke* as her primary personal brand.

**The lesson:** Build a personal brand (yourname.com, yourname on Instagram) alongside your business brand from day one. You'll eventually outgrow the niche brand. The personal brand travels with you everywhere.

## Same Photo Everywhere

> "You are your logo. I don't want to see a boring logo as your profile image because that doesn't work."

Brand recognition is built through repetition and consistency. Best Buy, Starbucks, Target, McDonald's — identical everywhere from Romania to California. Your face must work the same way.

**The rule:** One photo. Every platform. Every touchpoint.
- Online: Instagram, Facebook (personal + business page), YouTube, LinkedIn, email signature
- Physical: business cards, yard signs
- When your look changes significantly → update everywhere, all at once

**Background tip:** White background pops in email signatures. Colorful background makes you stand out in social media feeds. Same photo, adapted background if needed.

**You are not a logo** — you sell services, you are the brand. Every personal brand at scale (Tony Robbins, Gary Vee) is built around a face, not a symbol. The goal: when someone walks into a grocery store and sees you, they recognize you from your yard signs.

### How to Get Your Photo

Three options, from highest to lowest effort:

1. **Hire a photographer** — a few hundred dollars; best quality, professionally edited
2. **Teenager + phone + apps** — have someone take a good photo of you, then edit with:
   - **Darkroom** (app)
   - **Perfect365** (app)
   - Or run it through an Instagram filter
3. **AI avatar** — Higgsfield (see below) or **HeyGen** (upcoming GSMC module by [[Laura Kurz]])

### Plug It Into the Brand Tracker

Once you have your photo, add the link to the headshot field in your Brand Tracker doc (see [[Personal Branding#The Brand Tracker Document]]). Everything in one place.

## AI Headshots

Professional headshots are a required field in the Brand Tracker — and you don't need a photographer to get them. [[Laura Kurz]] taught this workflow inside *Gogo's Social Media Challenge 2026*:

**Tool:** [Higgsfield](https://higgsfield.ai)

**Step-by-step:**
1. Go to Higgsfield → Image → **Soul ID Character**
2. Upload **30–40 photos of yourself** to train your avatar twin
   - Selfies work; best method: record a short video of yourself talking, then screenshot frames — naturally captures varied expressions, movements, and hand gestures
3. Download the **Higgsfield Reference Extension** (browser extension, linked at the bottom of the Higgsfield site)
4. Go to **Pinterest** → search "professional real estate headshot" (or any style/pose you want)
5. Hover over any Pinterest image → green **Recreate** button appears
6. Click Recreate → select your avatar → set aspect ratio **9:16** → Generate
7. View all results in Higgsfield under **Browse Assets**

**Output:** Professional-quality headshot in any style, pose, or setting — ready to drop into social profiles, your Brand Tracker, and marketing materials.

## Your Bio

> "As you are growing and achieving new things, you're going to forget things."

Keep a **master bio** running in the background at all times — a document that captures every achievement, feature, speaking engagement, and product launch as it happens. This is the source of truth for all platform bios.

**6-month review:** Set a recurring calendar reminder. Read the bio, remove anything no longer relevant (old coaching offers, expired links, stale titles), and add what's new. A stale bio signals that you're not on top of your brand.

### The 3-Bio Framework

You need three distinct bios, each optimized for a different audience and context:

| Bio | Purpose | Used For |
|-----|---------|----------|
| **A — Real Estate** | Buyer/seller focused | Listing appointments, seller websites, buyer consultations |
| **B — Agent Attraction** | Production + eXp value prop | Recruiting, agent-facing speaking, GoGet'Em promotion |
| **C — Blended** | A + B combined | Podcasts, speaking engagements, stage presence |
| **D — Platform-Optimized** | Shortened versions of A, B, or C | Instagram, Facebook, LinkedIn profiles |

**Platform character limits:**
| Platform | Limit |
|----------|-------|
| Instagram | 150 characters |
| Facebook | 255 characters |
| LinkedIn | 220 characters |

### ChatGPT Bio Workflow

**Step 1 — Build Bio A (Real Estate):**
Use the ChatGPT prompt template (linked in the GSMC lesson): copy → paste into ChatGPT → answer the questions it asks → receive your bio.

**Step 2 — Build Bio B (Agent Attraction):**
Same process with the agent attraction prompt template.

**Step 3 — Build Bio C (Blended):**
Paste Bio A + Bio B into ChatGPT → prompt: *"Blend these two bios into one comprehensive About Me bio."*

**Step 4 — Build Bio D (Platform versions):**
Paste the relevant bio → prompt: *"Shorten this to fit Instagram's 150-character limit"* (or Facebook's 255, LinkedIn's 220).

**Step 5 — Clean it up:**
Remove the AI signature (the long em-dash ChatGPT appends) before publishing anywhere. Looks unprofessional if left in.

**Time investment:** ~1 day to complete all four versions.

### Brand Tracker Tie-in

Once finalized, add the direct link to each bio version into your Brand Tracker. You'll never hunt for your bio again — it's always one click away.

## Business Email (Google Workspace)

*(Walkthrough by [[Kristy Waker]], GSMC 2026. Follows domain purchase; precedes further brand infrastructure.)*

**Prerequisite:** Own your domain first. The business email must be tied to it.

**Why Google, not Microsoft:** The majority of realtors run on Google Workspace — Docs, Sheets, Calendar, Gmail all mesh together. Microsoft accounts create friction with shared tools.

**Brand Tracker row 12** = business/company email field. Log it here once set up.

### Setup Steps

1. Go to **Google Workspace** → choose **Starter plan** (14-day free trial, 30 GB storage, ~$6/month). Upgrade to Standard only when you need more storage.
2. Select **"Set up using an existing domain"** — enter your business domain (e.g., christysrealestateteam.com)
3. Create your username (e.g., `christy@christysrealestateteam.com`) and password
4. **Admin email = a personal address you'll have forever.** Never use a brokerage email — you could lose access when you change brokerages.
5. Verify domain ownership via **GoDaddy**: click "Sign in to GoDaddy" → Google connects automatically
6. Set up DNS settings to activate Gmail. DNS metaphor: *domain = your address, website = the house, DNS = the GPS telling everything where to go.*
7. Gmail may take up to **24 hours** to go live — monitor both old and new email during transition.

### After Setup

- **Enable two-step verification immediately** (Admin Console → Security). Apply to every user. Do not turn it off — re-enabling is painful.
- Upload your **business logo** to the Admin Console — appears in the top-right corner of your Google profile.
- Add the **same headshot** you use everywhere to your business profile.
- Create a **separate Chrome profile** for the business email — keeps business bookmarks, passwords, and sessions fully separate from personal.
- "**Done With You**" program available for agents who need hands-on help.

## Instagram Account Creation

*(Walkthrough by [[Kristy Waker]], GSMC 2026. Do this before setting up your profile links and bio.)*

**From your phone (recommended — more settings available):**

2. Name it after your business brand (e.g., christysrealestateteam)

**Meta Verified:** Not available immediately on new accounts. Requires a fully set-up profile and Meta Business Suite. Check eligibility after completing setup.

## Instagram Profile Setup

*(GSMC 2026 walkthrough.)*

**Edit Profile:** Instagram → bottom-right profile icon → **Edit Profile**.

**Bio:** Tap the bio field → paste your short bio. Who you are, what you sell, your niche. One clear message — no confusion about what you do.

**Links:**
- Unverified account: up to **2 links**
- Verified account: up to **5 links** — five entry points to your listings, website, lead magnets, and other platforms
- Each link can have a custom **title** and an **icon** (logo, image, or emoji) displayed next to it
- Tap Add Link → enter URL → add title → add icon → Done
- Log all links in your **Brand Tracker** so they're always one click away

**Contact Options** (Edit Profile → Profile Information → Contact Options):
- Business email
- Business phone number (choose Call or Text) — use your **BoldTrail smart number**, not personal cell
- WhatsApp phone number

**What visitors see:** Bio → Links → Contact button (tapping it reveals your email and phone options).

## Facebook Business Page

A Facebook Business Page is separate from your personal profile — you manage it *through* your personal account without ever logging out. Team members can also be granted access and manage the page from their own personal accounts — no shared passwords, no security issues.

### Creating from Scratch

1. Log into Facebook with your **personal account**
2. Click the **9-dot menu** (top right) → **Create** → **Page**
3. Enter your **page name** and set category to Real Estate
4. **Skip the bio** for now — complete the bio module first
5. Skip optional fields (website, location, hours, WhatsApp) — click through
6. Page is created — but you're not done yet

**Immediately set your username** (step most people miss):
7. Settings → Page Setup → Edit username
8. Type your brand handle → confirm available → Save
9. Your URL is now `facebook.com/yourusername` instead of an auto-generated string
10. Copy the clean URL → paste into your **Brand Tracker** (Facebook link field)

### Name vs. Username

- **Username** = appears in the URL. Must match your Instagram, TikTok, YouTube, and LinkedIn handles exactly.
- **Name** = what displays on the page. Less critical, but keep it clean.

The page name is set during creation. The username is set separately — don't skip this step.

### Updating an Existing Page

Settings → Page Setup → Edit name or username.

**Limits:** Page name can only be changed once every 60 days (Facebook reviews it). Username can be changed freely.

### Adding Team Access

Settings → Page Access → Add New → search by name. The team member logs into their own personal Facebook account and switches to the business page — clean separation, no shared passwords.

## Smart Phone Number (BoldTrail/KVCore)

*(Walkthrough by [[Kristy Waker]], GSMC 2026. KVCore has been rebranded as BoldTrail — same platform.)*

**The danger of the default eXp number:** The brokerage-provided number is a shared round-robin line. Any call from someone not already in your database routes to the next available agent in the office — your leads go to other agents. Do not use it for marketing.

**Buy your own smart number: ~$27/month** via BoldTrail → Profile → Phone → Buy Now.

**What it does:**
- Rings directly to you and only you
- Automatically adds every caller to your BoldTrail database as a new lead
- Texts route directly into your lead inbox
- Supports yard sign riders ("Dial 123 for more info on this property")

**Setup:** Set "Forward Agent Replies" to your personal cell number. Choose a **local area code** — leads trust and answer local numbers.

**Use it everywhere (Brand Tracker → smartphone number field):**
Yard signs, sign riders, website, KVCore IDX site, email signatures, Facebook, Instagram, business cards.

> Every CRM has this feature. If not on BoldTrail, get a smart number through whatever CRM you use.

## Social Media Covers (Canva)

*(GSMC 2026 training.)*

Every platform profile needs a cover image. Build once in Canva, resize for all platforms.

**Required elements in every cover:**
- Professional headshot
- Brand colors and visual elements
- Your logo + brokerage logo
- Website URL
- Business email
- QR code (recommended)

To download for multi-platform use: Share (top right) → Download → File type: PNG → check "Transparent background" → Download. The transparent PNG can be placed on any design on any platform without a white box behind it. (Tutorial: Ellen, Gogo's lead designer)

**Customization:**
- Change colors: click element → color picker in top bar
- Swap images: right-click → "Detach image" → Uploads → drag replacement into frame
- Edit text: double-click → retype → update color via top bar

**Download:** Always export as **PNG**. Share → Download → PNG → select page → Download.

**Finding templates:** Search "Facebook cover," "LinkedIn cover," or "real estate" in the Templates panel to start from a relevant base.

## YouTube Channel Setup

*(Two-part walkthrough by [[Kristy Waker]], GSMC 2026. Follows Facebook Business Page setup in the brand build sequence.)*

**Creating the channel:**
1. Go to YouTube → click your profile icon (top right) → **"View your channel"**
2. Click **"Customize channel"** to enter edit mode
3. Set your **channel name** — matches your business brand (e.g., Kristy's Real Estate Team)
4. Set your **custom handle** — this becomes your URL: `youtube.com/@YourHandle`. Must match your handle everywhere else.
5. Copy the handle URL → paste into your **Brand Tracker** (YouTube field)

**Customizing the channel:**
- **Banner image** — same banner used on Facebook, website, and LinkedIn. Consistent across all platforms.
- **Profile photo** — same headshot you use everywhere.
- **Description** — short bio. Adapt your Instagram bio for YouTube.
- **Links section** — add your website(s) and branded email. These display publicly on your channel page.
- **Video watermark** — upload your logo/icon. It appears in the corner of every uploaded video. Must be a small file size.

**Home tab layout:**
- Turn on the Home tab to customize your channel's visual layout — up to 12 sections.
- Available sections: Videos, Shorts, Playlists, Live Streams, Upcoming Live Streams, Featured Channels, Community.
- Reorder sections by dragging — put your best content type first.
- Remove sections you don't use yet (e.g., Live Streams if you don't go live).

**YouTube Studio:**
- Accessed at studio.youtube.com or via "Customize channel" → notice the URL shifts to YouTube Studio.
- Dashboard shows analytics, uploads, and content management once you start publishing.

**Granting team access:**
- YouTube Studio → **Settings → Permissions** → invite by email address → assign role (Manager, Editor, Viewer, etc.)
- Manager = full access short of deleting the channel
- Use this to give your VA or team member access without sharing your Google password

> "That's what's so nice about having that brand tracker — I'm going to go there and I grab the link."

**The rule:** Set up the infrastructure now. Upload banner, photo, and links once you have them. Don't wait for perfect — claim the handle and log it in the Brand Tracker today.

## LinkedIn Profile

*(Walkthrough by [[Kristy Waker]], GSMC 2026.)*

LinkedIn follows domain, Instagram, Facebook, and business email in the GSMC brand build sequence. Use a **two-pass approach:**

**Pass 1 — Secure and log (do this now):**
1. Go to LinkedIn.com → log in or create a new profile
2. Click your name or "View Profile" → click the **pencil icon** on the right side
3. Edit your custom URL → set it to your name (e.g., `linkedin.com/in/christywaker`) — not "real estate team" for a personal profile
4. Paste the clean URL into your **Brand Tracker** → check it off

- **Call Out:** Validate their effort, diagnose the real issue — *"You are doing the work, posting constantly. But you're still not getting leads. It's not your content — it's that you're speaking to the wrong people."*
- **Authority:** Prove you've lived it and have the answer — *"I built my real estate empire using this exact strategy."*

Opens by going against popular belief — creates a pattern interrupt.

- Tell the actual story, start to finish, in your own words

## Website Homepage Structure

> "When you're building a website, what do you want? You want a nice header. You want that landing page that tells you exactly what this website is about without scrolling. You want a clear call to action. You want a description. All of these things — put them in order."

Eight elements that every homepage needs, in the order they should appear. Not every website requires all 8 — the complexity of what you're selling determines which to include.

**1. Above the Fold**
What visitors see *without scrolling*. Must answer "what is this?" in one glance.
Two mandatory checks: ① Does it explain the page instantly? ② Is the **mobile version** also optimized? (Developers always do desktop first and forget mobile.)

**2. Clear Outcome Statement**
Plain language. No big words. What will the visitor get? Gogo's Circle: *"High-level limited seats mastermind for six-figure entrepreneurs to scale to seven figures."*

**3. Who Is It For / Who Is It NOT For**
Start with who it's *NOT* for — this filters unqualified leads before they waste your time on calls. Gogo's Circle opens with: *"If your goal is to learn how to close your next deal, this is not the place for you."* Only the right person reads past that line.

**4. Call to Action**
One button. One action. Crystal clear. Assume the visitor has never heard of you.
- *"Let's Talk eXp"* — eXp partner page
- *"Apply Now"* — The Circle coaching

**5. SEO Optimization**
Think: "If someone had never heard of me and Googled for what I do, what would they type?" That exact phrase must appear in your copy.
- Realtor example: "best realtor in Brighton Michigan" → must appear verbatim somewhere on your site
- Google spiders scan your text and surface you based on those words existing in that order
- *Gogo's live mistake:* Her Circle coaching page never once used "coach" or "coaching" — spotted mid-video, immediately flagged to her team. "That's very stupid. This is going to get redone."

**6. Header**
The top visual of the page — your photo, name, brand. Two options:
- Static image (most common, clean)
- Interactive/animated (Gogo's gobethke.com — she finds static boring)

**7. Video** *(conditional)*
Required for high-stakes or complex decisions. Optional for simple products the visitor already understands. Gogo's eXp partner page has 4–5 mandatory videos:
- Switching brokerages is life-changing — agents shouldn't do it just because she said so
- Each presentation is 1–2 hours; she can't repeat it thousands of times
- Agents watch at their own pace and self-qualify; by the time they book, the sale is mostly done
- *"Saves six hours of my life. They watch it whenever they are ready to make the switch."*

**8. FAQ Section** *(conditional)*
Only include if you keep answering the same questions. Proactively handles objections so people can decide without contacting you. Best for digital products, memberships, and service pages where the same 3–5 questions come up every time.

**Testimonials** *(place ~75% down the page)*
Social proof for the undecided. Video testimonials are strongest (but hard to collect). Written reviews from Google/Facebook pulled in as images work fine. *"If Casey and Deborah made this decision, I should too."*

**SEO homework:** Search Google for what you do — not your name. "Best realtor in [City, State]." Does your site come up? Does that phrase appear in your copy? If not, add it.

## The 5-Element Copywriting Framework

Universal structure for every piece of copy — social media post, email, website page, digital product page, or any content regardless of audience (sellers, buyers, or agents).

> "As long as your copywriting answers all of these questions, you're good to go."

| # | Element | What it does |
|---|---------|-------------|

| 2 | **Problem** | Names their problem bluntly — one line, out in the open |
| 3 | **Amplify Pain** | Makes the cost of inaction real — what happens if they don't fix this? |
| 4 | **Solution** | Clear, specific fix — what they need to do or what you offer |

**Flip it for any audience:** Same framework works for seller content, buyer content, or agent attraction — just change who you're talking to and what pebble is in their shoe.

**The three rules:**
- Never present a problem without a solution
- Never present a solution without first naming the problem
- Never post without a call to action

The GoGet'Em training provides a script. Answer the questions → ChatGPT outputs 10 concise sentences about you.

**Step 2 — Shoot One Sentence at a Time**
- Read each ChatGPT sentence → absorb it → say it in your own words → film
- Do NOT read word-for-word; speak naturally
- B-roll footage with voiceover can replace some clips
- One sentence per clip — brevity is the whole point

**Step 3 — Edit Into One Reel and Pin**
- Assemble all clips into a single Reel
- End with a clear CTA (*"Follow me if you're building something big"*)
- After posting: tap three dots → **"Pin to your profile"**
- It lives there permanently as one of your 3 pinned posts

**Gogo's own mistake:** Her first Root Story was 17 minutes. Laura flagged it; she had to redo it. Use ChatGPT to force conciseness.

Too many options = overwhelm = lost prospect. Narrow to the **top 3 ways you make money.** Common structures for real estate agents:

## Testimonial Strategy

*Source: [[Getting Video Testimonials from Clients (Gogo Bethke)]]*

> "You're not just talking about yourself — it's coming from other people, reassuring that you're not only doing a lot of transactions, but you're a good human."

Video testimonials are a core personal branding tool. They shift the conversation from self-promotion to social proof.

### Key Principles
- **Go to every closing** — catch clients at their happiest moment
- **Always under contract** — no contract, no work. *"People will take your business seriously when you take your business seriously."* Horror stories of clients switching agents "never happened to me because they were under contract."
- **Marketing release form at the same time as the buyer's agency** — face/image/voice/likeness covered forever. No permission friction at closings, inspections, or events.
- **Incentivize:** appraisal waivers, dinner gift cards, happy hour. "Throw them a bone."
- **Keep testimonials running:** Build a dedicated testimonials highlight on Instagram; tag clients so they share

### 6 Testimonial Formats
1. **Closing Table Moment** — selfie-style at signing, B-roll + talking head
2. **Client Couch Talk** — 3 questions via text, vertical video from home, edit to under 1.5 min
3. **"Why Did You Choose Me?"** — powerful for attracting new clients who see *why* people pick you
4. **Before & After** — horror stories from previous agents + transformation working with you
5. **Home Tour Celebration** — return 2 weeks after move-in, before/after property comparison
6. **Office Invite** — podcast-style interview + dinner/happy hour after

### Five-Star Review Script

> "I had a five-star experience working with you. I hope you had the same. Would you please take five minutes out of your time and leave me a review on these three platforms, because this is how I get to feed my family next month, too?"

**Why this works:** "Five-star" is planted in their mind before they open the review page — they arrive pre-framed.

**Delivery — two simultaneous hits:**
1. **Text** immediately after walking out of closing — links to Google, Facebook, and Zillow pre-saved in your Notes app
2. **Automated email** fires the moment your TC marks the file "closed" — same script, same three links

## Business Page vs. Personal Page

*Source: [[GoGet'Em Social Media Challenge Kickoff — Community Training]]*

> "You are limiting yourself. You will never have more than 5,000 followers. You're never going to be able to run an ad. And you're never going to have a private life if you're going to turn your private account into public."

Non-negotiable for anyone running a real estate business on social media. Running from a personal account creates a hard ceiling of 5,000 followers, eliminates the ability to run ads, and permanently exposes your private life.

**The system:**
- Business happens on the **business page** — posts, listings, market updates, all content
- Then **share it to your personal** — personal friends still see everything
- Both audiences captured; neither audience is sacrificed
- Business treated like a business; audience takes you seriously

**Business page advantages:** Unlimited followers, ads capability, public/private control, professional category designation, team access.

## Brand Naming Validation (Before You Commit)

*Source: [[GoGet'Em Social Media Challenge Kickoff — Community Training]]*

> "On Instagram and a .com, you can only have one and we need to own that."

Before committing to any brand name — business name, coaching program, digital product, speaking brand — run the two-step validation:

1. **Instagram:** Search the exact name. If nothing pops up, it's available.
2. **GoDaddy.com:** Check the same name with .com extension.
3. **If BOTH are available → that is your brand.** Buy the .com immediately and lock in the Instagram handle.

**Why these two matter:** On Facebook, duplicate names can coexist. On Instagram and .com, only one of each exists in the world. Once someone else owns either, you've either lost the asset entirely or will pay a premium to buy it back.

**Once cleared:** Buy the .com → build the website when ready → run the brand everywhere consistently. Own the asset first; build at whatever pace you need.

## Brand Consistency — The Target/Starbucks Lesson

*Source: [[GoGet'Em Brand Audit Call #3 — Stacey and Gina Brand Reviews]]*

> "Close your eyes and imagine Home Depot. Imagine Starbucks. Imagine Best Buy. Imagine Walmart. Why can you see them? Because that's a brand. It's the same in freaking Kentucky and it's the same in Virginia."

The most important brand concept Gogo teaches. You know exactly what you're looking at before you read a single word of copy — because the name, colors, logo, and photo are identical everywhere.

**For a real estate agent:** Same photo. Same colors. Same name. Every platform, every marketing piece, every yard sign, every card.

**The test:** When people see you walking into Target locally, do they recognize you and know what you do? If yes — that's a brand. If they'd have to ask — the brand isn't consistent enough yet.

**When you have a brand:** When people know (1) your name, (2) what you do, (3) where you do it, and (4) how to reach you — that's when you have a brand.

## QR Code Testing Rule

*Source: [[GoGet'Em Brand Audit Call — Amanda Richards and Angela Kane]]*

> "Don't send me a link unless you actually tried it. It's like, 'Oh, it's pretty.' Well, we don't need it to be pretty. We need it to work."

**The rule:** Before deploying ANY marketing material — physical or digital — test every link and QR code yourself, personally. Scan it. Click it. Confirm the destination is correct.

**Why it matters:** A broken QR code on a deployed piece means everyone who scans it gets sent somewhere wrong. Amanda's QR took people to lofty.com (her CRM homepage) instead of her personal website — ~80% of scanners got the wrong destination — due to a missing "https://" in the URL at generation time.

**Fix broken QR codes:** Regenerate via ChatGPT or the GoGet'Em tech support call. Confirm correct destination before printing/deploying.

**The principle applies to:** QR codes, website links, calendar buttons, CRM listing links, Linktree buttons, email links, everything. If it wasn't tested, it isn't done.

## Two-Button Link-in-Bio Strategy

*Source: [[GoGet'Em Brand Audit Call — Amanda Richards and Angela Kane]]*

> "When I click that button, I need to know what I'm signing up for."

Any agent doing both real estate AND agent attraction must separate their calendar links in their bio. A single generic "Book a Call" button:
- Fails to communicate what type of call is being booked
- Allows anyone (including random people with no intent) to book time
- Mixes real estate clients with agent attraction prospects in the same funnel

**The fix — two separate buttons:**
| Button | Destination |
|--------|------------|
| "Partner with me at eXp" | Agent attraction / eXp conversation calendar |
| "Real estate inquiry" | Buyer/seller consultation calendar |

**Result:** Each click self-selects the prospect type. The right conversation happens with the right person. No time-wasters. No confusion.

This applies to Linktree, Beacons, Instagram link section, or any link-in-bio tool.

## Serve, Don't Perform

*Source: [[GoGet'Em Training — Camera Confidence and Social Media Posting Workshop]]*

> "When you focus on serving instead of performing, confidence naturally increases."

One of Gogo's core on-camera principles — and a broader content philosophy. The difference between serving and performing is felt by the audience even through a screen.

**Performing:** Creating content to look good, seem impressive, or project an image.
**Serving:** Creating content because the person watching has a problem you can solve.

The reframe: instead of "I'm so excited to share this" → address the viewer's problem directly. Instead of making yourself the center, make the audience's outcome the center.

**Practical check for any piece of content:** Who is this really for — me or them?

## Brand Consistency — The "Target Test"

*Source: [[The Fundamentals - Social Media Machine and Branding (Gogo Bethke)]]*

> "Your brand is you. And you have to be exactly the same everywhere until people recognize you at the grocery store and they say, 'Oh my gosh, aren't you that go-go chick that sells real estate?'"

The bar for brand consistency is a physical-world recognition test: **same name, same bio, same current photo across every platform** — plus yard signs, email signature, and business cards — until strangers recognize you in the grocery store (the "Target test").

**Critical caveat:** the photo must **actually look like you today.** No decades-old graduation photo. If a stranger can't match the face on your feed to the face in the aisle, the whole consistency chain breaks.

This is the recognition mechanism behind [[Personal Branding#Same Photo Everywhere]] and [[Personal Branding#Same Username Everywhere]] — the Target test is simply the outcome those rules are engineered to produce.

## Own Your .com + Brand-vs-LLC Separation

*Source: [[The Fundamentals - Social Media Machine and Branding (Gogo Bethke)]]*

**Own yourname.com and your Instagram handle — check both *before* naming any brand.** Only one owner is possible on Instagram + .com; everywhere else (Facebook, LinkedIn) multiples can coexist. Domains are ~$11/year.

> "Owning a website, guys, is like $11 a year. If you can't afford $11 a year, take a night shift at Taco Bell, still buy your .com."

Buy your kids' .com too (see [[Personal Branding#Pro Tip: Buy Your Children's Names]]).

### Never Name Your LLC After Your Brand
Naming your LLC after your public brand "serves you up on a silver platter" to anyone who wants to sue you — they know exactly where to look to see what you own.

> "Do not name your LLC your brand… you just served it up on a silver platter to someone if they want to sue you. You know exactly where to find what you own."

**Brand ≠ LLC.** Hide assets via holding companies and trusts ("a box in a box in a box") using a firm like [[Anderson Advisors]]. Gogo holds **27 LLCs** under an unlimited plan.

## Hub-and-Spoke Domain Architecture

*Source: [[The Fundamentals - Social Media Machine and Branding (Gogo Bethke)]]*

> "All roads lead to yourname.com."

Every venture gets its own website, but each one **feeds back to the personal-name hub.** The personal domain is the center; the ventures are spokes.

**Gogo's model:** [[gogobethke.com]] is the hub. It funnels to — and is fed by — [[Gogo's Real Estate Team]], [[GoGet'Em Community]], [[The Circle (Coaching Program)]], the [[Gopreneur Podcast]], boot camps, and agent attraction. This is the branding counterpart to the [[Social Media and Email Marketing#The Social Media Machine — One-to-Many Funnel|one-to-many social funnel]]: all platforms and all ventures ultimately route back to the single personal hub, where the relationship (and the sale) closes.

## You Are the Brand, Not the Brokerage

*Source: [[What Top Agents Do Differently on Instagram - Month 5 (Gogo Bethke, Allison, Cat West)]]*

The panel's unanimous verdict: clients and recruits buy the *person*, never the company logo.

> "Nobody's buying EXP. Nobody. They're buying you. I don't care what company you're from." — [[Allison]]

[[Cat West]] applies the same logic to agent attraction — she deliberately repels wrong-fit followers so raving fans self-select in: *"I want those people who are not into who I am to leave, because the people who stay are going to be your raving fans."* Gogo's framing: *"Your client becomes the agent versus the buyers and sellers. - **One account, personal brand.** Never split personal/professional — promote team brand and community under your personal account; make the others collaborators.
- **Bio as storefront** — a clean three-line structure (who I am / who I help / lead magnet) beats a title salad.
- **Evolve your brand as your life phases change** — *"your soul will tell you when you've outgrown an era."*

## On-Camera Brand Presence

*Source: [[Social Media Marketing Mindset and Lead Capturing (Gogo Bethke)]]*

Your brand is not just how you look on paper — it's how you carry yourself on camera. The **10 camera-confidence principles** (full list in [[Social Media and Email Marketing#Camera Confidence — 10 Principles]]) are, at their core, brand-presence rules: look people in the eye, don't apologize before you speak, hold your posture like the expert you are, and above all **make it about them, not you.**

> "It's really not about the house. It's really about me and my services and my abilities to help you get into this house."

**On-brand automated messaging:** every auto-responder, ManyChat reply, and drip message must sound like *you* — CRMs ship generic copy that "screams system." Rewrite them in your voice so the person on the other end never realizes it's automated. Track what matters: the **brand tracker** logs views/followers per platform; the **lead tracker** logs leads per platform — because *"we want those views to equal US dollars in your bank account."*

## Brand Voice via Custom GPT

*Source: [[Master Your Copywriting and AI Brand Voice (Gogo Bethke)]]*

> "Copywriting is you in a written format. Period."

A consistent written voice is a brand asset — and it can be codified into AI. [[Kristy Waker]]'s custom GPT **"My AI Brand Voice"** captures your voice as a reusable profile so every piece of copy sounds like you, not a robot. (Full setup: [[Personal Branding#My AI Brand Voice — Custom GPT Setup]].)

**The rule that makes voice a brand:** sound like you, always — typos, accent, imperfect grammar and all. Gogo never fixes her quirks (*"if I change that, I lose my power"*). The custom GPT's job is to *preserve* that fingerprint at scale, then strip the AI tells (em dashes, buzzwords, uniform paragraphs — see [[Social Media and Email Marketing#AI-vs-Human Writing Tells (strip these out)]]) so the output reads as authentically human.

**Maintain the voice over time.** Revisit custom instructions every 1–2 years and correct the GPT live when it drifts — a brand voice that's frozen to a two-year-old version of you (old kid ages, stale bio) stops being consistent with the real you.

## Circle Coaching — Niche Ownership & Verified Delegation

*Source: [[The Circle Coaching — Niche Ownership, AI Execution Layer and Full-Service Delegation (de-identified)]]. Private [[The Circle|Circle]] coaching, de-identified — Gogo's generalizable teachings only.*

> "Stop trying to be everything to everyone."

**Own the niche you're already living — don't invent one.** When lived experience already forms a clear specialty, the move isn't to manufacture a new angle: "You don't need a new niche — you need to own the one you've been living." **Translate lived experience into authority** a competitor can't buy — lead with the personally-lived story, not a sales claim.

**Write positioning for the audience's knowledge, not yours.** Use the **broadest recognizable geographic/category label** the audience will actually know. If they won't recognize the hyper-local name, name the region — the point is to be found, not to be precise.

### Marketing-Release Testimonials

Testimonials are a branding asset only if you can *reuse* them — so capture and license accordingly:
- Ask for a **recorded video** focused on the **emotional arc** (how they felt, what changed), not a feature list.
- Post it as a **reel** → repurpose into a written **case study** → later run it as a **paid ad**.
- Get a **signed marketing release** (name, image, voice) up front so the testimonial can legally run as advertising down the line.

### Verified Media Delegation

So a VA can produce and post as the brand without sharing passwords or diluting the verified identity:
- **Meta Verified** for the account
- **Shared Access** (not shared logins) for team members
- **Curate approved media into a shared album** the VA pulls from — the brand stays consistent and the owner keeps control.
