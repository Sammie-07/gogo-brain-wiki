# Negotiation

Gogo's practical negotiation playbook, heavily influenced by Chris Voss's *Never Split the Difference* and her own experience as a direct, Eastern European-style communicator learning to add tactical empathy to her toolkit.

> "True ambition isn't about beating the other person. It's about beating who you were yesterday."

## Core Insight

Negotiation is not a special skill for big deals — it's happening constantly. The amygdala (fear brain) responds identically to a hostage situation and an argument about chores. That means hostage-negotiation techniques work in sales, team management, family, and anywhere humans interact.

## Three Conflict Styles (from Chris Voss)

Understanding your own type — and the other person's — determines which tactics to use.

| Style | Drive | Nickname | Pitfalls |
|-------|-------|----------|----------|
| **Assertive** | Respect, being heard | The fighter | Too transactional; forgets gratitude; people feel like a means to an end |
| **Analyst** | Data, certainty | The flighter | Paralysis by analysis; hates surprises; appears cold/detached |
| **Accommodator** | Harmony | The friend-maker | Avoids hard truths; hope-based; creates "train wrecks" by not raising problems |

**Gogo identifies as primarily Assertive.** Over time she deliberately developed Analyst habits (data/trackers/decisions by numbers) and Accommodator habits (expressing gratitude, learned from her husband's more openly appreciative American upbringing).

**Richard Branson** is cited as a classic Accommodator who compensates by building systems and teams.

## Key Tactics

### Tactical Empathy
Not sympathy — not feeling bad for someone. Just the cognitive ability to identify what they're feeling and say it out loud.

- You don't need to agree with the emotion, own it, or feel it yourself.
- Simply: **identify → articulate.**
- Gogo used to have the "weebie-jeebies" around empathy (Eastern European upbringing; feelings = irrelevant). She used to inject empathy into her writing with ChatGPT because it wasn't her natural default.

### Mirroring
Repeat the last **3 words** someone said back to them as a question.

- Lowest-effort rapport-building technique.
- Forces the other person to elaborate and feel heard.
- Buys time and builds connection without requiring emotional investment.

### The Magic Wand Effect
Proactively name the negative emotion you think the other side has.

> "It seems like you feel I slapped you in the face with that offer."

- Counterintuitive: naming the negative doesn't make it worse — it diffuses it.
- When you voice their hidden thought, they feel seen. The negativity often evaporates.
- You must make them feel understood before they can hear your perspective.

### Let the Other Side Have Your Way
Instead of telling them what to do, ask open-ended **how/what** questions.

- Guide them toward the solution you want — let them propose it.
- You get to choose the best option from their suggestions.
- Like a baseball batter watching pitches: you're not swinging at everything, you're waiting for the right pitch.
- Shifts from confrontation → guided collaboration.

### Staying Calm Under Pressure
- **Visualize** yourself responding calmly in advance. Re-shoot the movie in your head.
- Most people mentally rehearse getting angry — you practice the negative reaction. Stop. Rehearse the calm one.
- **Smile while on the phone** — it actually changes your tone and physiology.
- Use the **escape hatch phrase**: "Let me think about it." (Credit: Barbara Corcoran) — buys time, prevents instant emotional reactions you'll regret.

### Framing Difficult News
**"I found something fascinating"** — the phrase Gogo's team uses when delivering bad news to her.

- Reframes the difficult thing as an interesting problem to solve, not an attack or crisis.
- Disarms her Assertive default before she has a chance to react defensively.

### Welcome Black Swans (Voss)
Any unexpected surprise in a negotiation = a **black swan**. They contain the most valuable information — hidden truths, real leverage points.

- If you're not being surprised, you're probably not listening hard enough.
- Surprises aren't obstacles; they're opportunities. Lean into them.

## Chris Voss — Full Session (Gogopreneur)

Chris Voss appeared on Gogopreneur to teach his full framework. Key additions beyond what appears above:

**The amygdala explanation:** The brain's threat response (amygdala) cannot distinguish between a saber-tooth tiger and a difficult negotiation. Both produce identical physiological responses. Tactical empathy bypasses this response by making the other person feel heard before any logic is introduced. Once the amygdala calms down, rationality returns.

**31% smarter in a positive frame (Sean Achor):** People in a positive emotional state are 31% more cognitively effective. Creating a positive emotional frame in negotiation literally makes your counterpart better at making decisions — decisions that benefit both parties.

**"I found something fascinating":** Gogo's own bad-news delivery protocol — she independently developed it; Chris Voss validated it on the episode. Opens with curiosity rather than alarm, preventing the emotional spike that makes bad news worse.

**Barbara Corcoran's line:** "Give me time to think about it." The most powerful response to any pressure moment. Never make a decision under pressure if you can create a pause first.

**Documentary:** *Tactical Empathy* — available on Amazon Prime.

**Black swans:** Any unexpected surprise in a negotiation contains the most valuable information. Lean into surprises rather than treating them as obstacles.

## Art of the Ask — Referral Negotiation (Brandon Barnum)

The three-step referral ask framework from Brandon Barnum applies negotiation principles to referral conversations:

1. **Set the stage** — prime the conversation before asking ("I have a favor to ask you in a moment")
2. **Listen for referral triggers** — signals that someone knows a potential client (moving mentions, life changes, job changes)
3. **Ask in peak referral state** — timing is everything; ask right after you've delivered a win or good news, when goodwill and reciprocity are at their highest

## "No Is Your Friend" — Buyer Closing and Objection Framework

From an anonymous GoGet'Em presenter who applied Chris Voss principles directly to real estate buyer consultations and showings:

**Core reframe:** "No" in a real estate context is not rejection — it is information. Every no reveals a constraint, a fear, a preference. When a buyer says no, they are telling you exactly what they actually need.

**Outside-in showing strategy:**
- Never show the best home first
- Start with homes that are slightly below what the buyer wants (too small, wrong neighborhood, slightly dated)
- By the third or fourth showing, the buyer has calibrated against lower options and is primed to recognize value
- Works with anchoring psychology: the "good" house feels dramatically better after the "adequate" houses

**Handling "we want to think about it":**
- Mirror: "You want to think about it?"
- Then: "What specifically is giving you pause?" 
- This opens the real objection — which is never "we want to think about it"

**The "No" re-label:**
- Train yourself to hear "no" as "not yet" or "not this one"
- Each no is a data point that narrows the search and builds trust
- The buyer who says no twice and then yes is a stronger lead than the one who says yes immediately

**Buyer agreement tie-in:** Without a signed buyer agreement, every conversation where a buyer says "no" and walks away costs you your time with no recourse. The agreement converts a casual showing into a professional engagement.
