# Communication

The GoGet'Em community's frameworks for communication span three distinct but overlapping domains: **conscious communication** (Jamie Miller), **influence science** (René Rodriguez), and **leadership presence** (Ludmila Woodruff). Together they form a comprehensive framework for how real estate professionals communicate with clients, teams, and themselves.

## Conscious vs. Unconscious Communication (Jamie Miller)

Most people communicate on autopilot — default patterns installed in childhood, activated under stress. These patterns feel natural because they're familiar, not because they're effective.

**Conscious communication = deliberate choice.** Words, tone, pacing, and presence chosen intentionally rather than triggered automatically.

**Why this matters in business:**
- Clients make emotional decisions before rational ones; the way you say something shapes how they hear it
- Team culture is built from the cumulative effect of everyday communication — not from policies
- Psychological safety (which determines whether your team performs at a high level) is created or destroyed through consistent leadership language

**Awareness before change.** You cannot change what you don't see. The first step is identifying your patterns — noticing when you're on autopilot, what triggers default responses, and what you say when you're stressed vs. when you're at your best.

**Words create reality.** "I *have to* prospect" vs. "I *get to* connect with people today" — identical action, different emotional reality. Language shapes motivation, effort quality, and results.

**Self-communication.** The internal voice narrating your experience also follows conscious vs. unconscious patterns. Self-talk under stress defaults to the least-conscious (most critical, most fearful) pattern. Applying conscious communication to your own inner dialogue is the foundation; you cannot speak consciously to others if you're in an unconscious internal state.

**Format.** Jamie Miller teaches interactively — teach a principle, then open for real-time questions on specific situations participants face. The questions of the group become the curriculum.

## Influence Science (René Rodriguez)

Influence is the root skill underneath all other business skills — selling, leading, negotiating, parenting, presenting. Everything is downstream from your ability to move people.

**Behavioral neuroscience applied to communication.** Human decision-making is predictable and follows brain biology, not logic. Understanding how the brain processes risk, trust, and value allows you to communicate in alignment with how people actually decide — not how they think they decide.

**Authentic vs. manipulative influence.** The distinction matters:
- **Authentic influence:** serves the person being influenced; creates genuine buy-in; builds lasting trust
- **Manipulation:** serves only the influencer; creates surface-level compliance; erodes trust over time

For real estate professionals, every listing presentation, buyer consultation, and negotiation is an influence event. Agents who understand influence science close more consistently and ethically.

> "Influence is the root skill." — René Rodriguez's opening premise

## Leadership Communication and Presence (Ludmila Woodruff)

Effective leadership communication begins with the body, not the mind. Before words, there is presence. Before content, there is regulation.

**The nervous system determines what gets heard.** When a leader communicates from a dysregulated state (stress, frustration, urgency), those emotional signals transmit to the team before any words land. Team members' nervous systems respond to their leader's state — not just to what the leader says.

**Presence as the foundation of trust.** Psychological safety is created through consistent regulated presence, not through being nice or making accommodations. When a leader is present and grounded during hard conversations, the team learns: *this is a safe place to bring reality.*

**Emotional mastery.** Not suppression — intelligent awareness and direction of emotion. Leaders who understand their own emotional patterns can choose how to respond rather than defaulting to reaction. This is the difference between a leader who is honest and a leader who is reactive.

**The sequence:**
1. Regulate your own nervous system before entering high-stakes communication
2. Access genuine presence (not performed calm, but actual groundedness)
3. Communicate from that state — the content lands differently

## Communication in Real Estate Practice

Applying these frameworks in real estate contexts:

**With clients:**
- "I found something fascinating…" (Gogo's approach to bad news) opens with curiosity rather than alarm. See [[Mindset#"I Found Something Fascinating" (Bad News Protocol)]]
- Tone and framing shape how market data lands — the same statistic presented with confidence vs. anxiety produces different client decisions
- Listening before solving (understanding what the client actually wants to communicate, not just the words)

**With teams:**
- Team culture is built word by word, meeting by meeting; there is no neutral communication
- Clear agreements over vague expectations — ambiguity creates anxiety
- Accountability conversations done well (from a regulated, present state) strengthen trust; done poorly they create fear and avoidance

**With yourself:**
- The language you use about your work shapes your relationship to it
- "I have to make calls" vs. "I get to talk to people who need help" → same calls, different energy brought to them
- Morning self-talk and daily internal narration set the tone for everything that follows

## Relationship and Gratitude Outreach (Jon Pugle)

[[Jon Pugle]] applies communication as the core of sphere-of-influence business: the way you reach out — not just whether you reach out — determines whether relationships convert. His premise mirrors the leadership frameworks above but aimed outward at clients and referral partners: *"The antidote for AI is human connection."* Automations get replaced; genuinely human communication is the moat.

**Gratitude as the medium, not the ask.** Communicate to make people feel thought of, never to extract. Two signature tactics:
- **Handwritten notes** — 3–5 sentences, personal, no business card, no ask. They land precisely because they carry no agenda; recipients keep them on their desk.
- **30-second gratitude videos** — filmed specifically for one person (*"Hey Mike, thought of you, so grateful for you…"*), never a generic clip blasted to everyone. Specificity is what makes it feel like communication rather than marketing.

**"People participate in things they author."** The most durable buy-in comes from letting people co-create. Pugle's board-of-directors dinner asks top referrers to co-author his client events and vendor list — participation follows authorship. Treat influential-but-quiet people as if they already refer heavily and they grow into it. (Parallels [[#Influence Science (René Rodriguez)|authentic influence]] — serving the person being influenced rather than manipulating.)

> "People participate in things they author. Write that down." — Jon Pugle

**Situational-trigger outreach removes the "I don't know what to say" excuse.** Anchor the contact to a real-world reason so there's no pressure: *"driving by your neighborhood," "I'll be in your area Tuesday anyway," "I'm already showing a house nearby — want to piggyback?"* Telling people you'll already be there takes the weight off the ask. Mine their last three social posts and comment on something specific — the guessing game is over. (This is the relationship-side of the [[Lead Generation#Sphere-of-Influence System (Jon Pugle)|interest-based CRM tagging]] system.)

**Care communicates louder than pitch.** *"Treat people's children better than you treat them. They will love you absolutely forever."* Put clients' kids and pets at the forefront of every conversation — the message people actually hear is *you see what matters to me.*

## Listing Scripts — "I Don't Compete" & the Non-Negotiable Fee (Jeannie Steele)

*Source: [[Building a Dominant Business in a Small Market (Jeannie Steele)]]. [[Jeannie Steele]] on the listing conversation as a confidence and boundary exercise, not a persuasion one.*

**"I don't compete."** In 12+ years Jeannie competed for only 5 listings and won zero — and she's fine with that. Her stance, drawn from *The Full Fee Agent*: **if a seller is interviewing you, they've already chosen** the agent they want; the interview is theater. So she doesn't perform. She wishes them luck, keeps her marketing running, and moves on. Refusing to compete is itself the positioning — the agent who won't audition reads as the agent who doesn't need to.

**The non-negotiable-fee script.** Jeannie draws a hard line between two different fees. **Buyer-side compensation is negotiable**; **her listing fee is not.** When a seller asks "what will you take?" on her own fee, she stands up:

> "Mine's not negotiable. Take it or leave it. That's what I charge. If you don't like it, hire somebody else. I am not the agent for you." — Jeannie Steele

Naming yourself out of the deal ("I am not the agent for you") is the move — it hands the choice back to the seller and signals you're not the one who needs the sale.

**Speed is the edge.** Rather than argue value, she competes on velocity: *"I list very quickly — don't have a day, let's roll."* Being the fastest to get a home live is a differentiator no fee negotiation can match.

**The boundary line — "were we ever?"** When a friend who had secretly interviewed other agents behind her back asked whether they were still friends, Jeannie answered:

> "'Are we still friends?' And I said, 'Were we ever?'" — Jeannie Steele

The line illustrates the same non-negotiable posture applied to relationships: she doesn't chase people who don't value her, friend or client.
