# Productivity

[[Gogo Bethke]]'s system for ruthlessly protecting time by categorizing every task as either money-making (green light) or delegatable (red light), outsourcing everything that isn't in your lane, and tracking all activity so decisions are driven by data, not feelings.

> "Time is LIFE. We all have the same 24 hours in a day, but how we spend it separates the dreamers from the doers."

## Core Principle: Stay In Your Lane

> "If it feels like work, I'm hiring it out."

Money is made fastest in the activities you're naturally gifted at. Everything else costs more in your time than it earns, or can be done better by someone who loves doing it. Gogo runs a team of 14 specifically so she never has to touch red-light tasks.

> "Today I get paid to be me because I stay in my lane."

## Hourly Rate Formula

**Annual income ÷ 2,080 = your hourly rate.**

This number becomes your filter for every decision. Post it on sticky notes at the washer, kitchen sink, and desk — so every task you see triggers the question: *is this worth my hourly rate?*

Example: $250,000 ÷ 2,080 = **$120.19/hr.** Running laundry or grocery shopping doesn't save $25 — it costs $120 (the earnings you gave up by doing a red-light task yourself).

> "You are not saving the $25. You're technically losing $120 by doing your own laundry."

## The Leverage Hierarchy

Three levers for multiplying your time, always implemented in this order — cheapest first:

1. **Automations** — ManyChat, Go High Level, CRM sequences, email drip. Works 24/7, even while sleeping or on vacation. Cost: $20–200/month. *"Automations are your cheapest way to leverage. It is cheaper than a VA's time and it is definitely cheaper than your hourly rate."*
2. **Virtual assistants** — handles anything requiring a human but not your hourly rate. $3–6/hr offshore; ~$18–30/hr US in-person.
3. **In-person staff** — highest cost; necessary only for physical tasks (yard signs, closing gifts, house management).

Automate everything automatable before you hire a person. Only hire in-person staff when the task requires physical presence.

## The Red Light / Green Light Exercise

A physical audit of your entire day — every task, business or personal — categorized into two columns.

**How to run it:**
1. Carry a clipboard or dedicated notebook for a full day (from the moment you wake up)
2. Draw a vertical line down the middle of a page
3. Left column: **Red Light** — right column: **Green Light**
4. Every single thing you do gets recorded in one column

Run it for a **full quarter** to capture daily, weekly, monthly, and quarterly tasks — not just a snapshot day.

**Red Light = Stop**
- Needs to get done, but not by you
- Someone else can do it (an assistant, a contractor, a service)
- Examples: buying gifts, taking out signs, scheduling meetings, grocery shopping
- The question: *could I hire someone who loves this task to do it?*

**Green Light = Money**
- Only you can do it
- Generates revenue that exceeds what it would cost to hire someone
- Cannot be delegated (at least not yet)
- Examples: coaching calls, negotiating deals, social media content (for Gogo), client conversations

**Gogo's green lights** (as of time of recording):
- Coaching calls
- Negotiating deals and contracts
- Social media / content creation
- Writing emails (the actual content)
- Speaking engagements, board meetings, hosting events in person
- Your own photos/videos (voice and presence can't be delegated)

**Gogo's red lights:**
- Calendar management — she hasn't managed her own calendar in 7+ years
- Scheduling back-and-forth ("are you free Tuesday?")
- Admin tasks, sign logistics, gift buying
- Emails, social media scheduling, DMs/comments
- Appointment setting, graphics/flyers, data entry
- Yard signs, grocery shopping, office organization, event setup/breakdown

> "Your ego is not your amigo. It is not here to make you more money."

Don't let ego answer the red/green question — it will justify doing everything yourself "to save $25."

## Virtual Assistants

### The TC as First Hire

> "If you don't have an assistant, you are the assistant."

A Transaction Coordinator (TC) is Gogo's recommended **first hire** for any agent looking to scale. A TC manages all post-contract admin — opening escrow, tracking contingency dates, coordinating title/lender/other agent, preparing closing docs — freeing the agent for dollar-productive work. Typical cost: $300–$500/closing (per-transaction, no salary risk). Start virtual to test workflow before committing to full-time.

### The 560-Hours-in-40 Framework

By managing a team of VAs handling concurrent tasks (social media, admin, follow-up, content, lead tracking), one person produces what would take 560 hours alone — while personally working only 40. This is the mathematical argument for building a VA team rather than doing it yourself.

**Full leverage math (BBM Retreat Day 2 version):**

| Component | Hours/Week | Hours/Year |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| 14 VAs × 40 hrs | 560 | 29,120 |
| + Gogo's personal 24 hrs (Mon–Wed) | 24 | 1,248 |
| **Total** | **584** | **30,368** |

Average person works 2,080 hrs/year. Gogo's system produces **10.8× more output** than a solo operator.

**Revenue result:** Gogo makes **$2.7M working 24 hours/week**. Cannot remember the last time she answered an email, a DM, or put something in her own calendar.

**Before the VA team:** Made $800K/year with one assistant, working 40 hrs/week with her "head spinning." Revenue more than tripled; personal hours dropped by 40%.

**VA math pitch:** 14 VAs × ~$4/hr = $56/hour for 14 concurrent hours of work. "If I said 'Give me $56 and I give you 14 hours worth of work done' — would you do it?"

## CEO Muscle (Beth Manning)

> "The CEO muscle is built doing the boring important things everyone else avoids."

Like any muscle, the CEO muscle atrophies without use. It's built through consistent attention to financials, planning, and systems review — even when those activities feel unglamorous. It cannot be built through busyness alone; it requires deliberate work *on* the business.

**QuickBooks over Excel:** Gogo's reaction to agents who use Excel for their financials: "Oh hell no." QuickBooks (or equivalent accounting software) is non-negotiable for any agent running a real business. Track every dollar with a real tool.

**Specificity in numbers:** The CEO doesn't say "I want to do more deals." The CEO says "I want to close 24 deals at an average sale price of $350K." The same agent producing 24 transactions at $200K vs. $500K has dramatically different income — and most agents never consciously choose their average sale price. CEOs do.

**Beth Manning's trajectory:** Started at $2.7M in sales. Reached $10M primarily by doubling her average sale price — not by doubling her transaction count. The smarter path.

## TC 10-Transaction Rule (Melissa Vinke)

> "After your first ten transactions — five each side — you should never process your own paper again."

Gogo's rule: agents must handle their first 10 transactions themselves (5 as buyer's agent, 5 as listing agent). Learn the process before you delegate it. After 10: hire a TC immediately.

**What a TC handles:** Inspections, deposits, wiring, intro emails to all parties, deadline tracking, coordination with title and lender. All of it.

**Ideal client for TC services:** Agents doing 3–5 deals per month who are ready to stop pushing paper.

**One dedicated TC per agent:** Relationship-based model. One TC learns your style, clients, and preferences. No rotating reps.

**Introduction language:** "This is [TC name] who manages my transactions so I don't push paper." Positions the TC as a professional asset.

## Hire Your Opposite (Coleen Otero)

> "Hire your opposite. You don't need a mini-me — you need someone who fills your gaps."

Don't hire a second version of yourself. Hire for the skills and personality traits you lack. A visionary hires a detail person. An introvert hires an extroverted connector. The goal is complementarity, not confirmation.

## Time > Money at Scale (Zee Slingsby)

At a certain income level, time becomes scarcer than money. When that threshold is crossed, designing your offers around time protection (group programs, digital products, recurring models) becomes more important than optimizing for revenue per hour.

The shift: from "how do I earn more per transaction?" to "how do I serve more people without proportionally more hours?"

## Pre-Launch Selling Strategy (Zee Slingsby)

The most productive way to build a digital product or course: start marketing before it is finished. Sell before you're done.

**Why it works:**
- Marketing is energizing when you are excited about the idea -- before you've built it and hit friction
- If someone buys before you finish, the fire is lit under you to complete it
- If nobody buys yet, you have no emotional sunk cost from an unfinished product
- Gogo's bootcamp social media course started at $49 pre-launch; sold for $3,000; early buyers kept $49 forever

**How to execute:**
1. Create an RSVP landing page on any email platform to collect interested names and emails
2. Offer founding member / early-bird pricing for pre-launch buyers
3. Disclose clearly: "Access available on [date] -- early price for trusting me now"
4. Follow the 30-day pre-launch story guide: a daily checklist of what to talk about on Stories during pre-launch

**Zee's track record using this method:** Launched all her courses on early bird -- sold before they were done -- across four signature courses, six mini-courses, and four masterminds.

**30-day course creation is realistic:** A full course can be built in 30 days when you give yourself a deadline. Set the goal; take the action. "One of these days" without a deadline becomes "probably never."

**Stan Store as platform:** Easiest to get started same day. MP4 upload directly from Zoom or Loom -- no reformatting needed. 30% higher conversion than other platforms per Amy Gregory. $99/month or free trial.

## VA Hiring Process (Full SOP)

From Biz Mastery Week 1 — Gogo's documented step-by-step:

1. **Red Light / Green Light exercise** first — build the job description from the list
2. **Create a Google Form** for applicants; attach to a spreadsheet for tracking
3. **Post on VA / freelancer Facebook groups** — join as many as possible; post job, wait for admin approval, applications flow in
4. **Email template** for all applicants — saves time and feels professional; ask for:
   - Results of two DISC assessments (standard DISC + Tony Robbins DISC)
   - Work samples if relevant (video, design, etc.)
   - A short intro video — reveals personality and English level
5. **Color-code applicants:** green = strong fit, red = no, yellow = unsure; add Notes column per row
6. **Interview promising candidates** — schedule via Calendly, send calendar invite
7. **After decision to hire:**
   - Send contract for e-sign
   - Have them attend all team meetings for 2-4 weeks to understand the full business
   - Give tasks list / SOPs → week 1: watch you do it → week 2: they do it, you watch → week 3: they do it alone
8. **Three-month evaluation period** → keep or release
9. **Remove all access immediately** if releasing

**Referral tip:** Ask current VAs for referrals — they know the work and will vouch for quality.
**Delegation option:** A senior VA can run the entire hiring process — you just provide the ideal profile.

> "They are going to get away with whatever you allow."

## Parallel VA Training

Never hire just one VA. Train two simultaneously -- same meetings, same onboarding, same process. If one quits, gets sick, or is slow in learning, the other covers. The training cost is essentially the same (one more person on a Zoom call). Minimum: $700/month for two part-time VAs.

If hiring couples as VAs: works only if you have 3+ VAs total. If you only have two and they're a couple, shared vacations double your coverage gap.

Applied by Gogo to GoGet'Em webinar checkout pages.

**eXp closes monthly data** after the month ends -- you cannot go back and retrieve historical agent counts or tier breakdowns. This tracker must be maintained in real time or the data is lost permanently.

**Projections:** Once average monthly growth % is established, the spreadsheet projects forward. Knowing your compounding rate lets you calculate income in 12, 24, 36 months -- turning goal-setting from guessing into math.

> "As soon as you can imagine it, it's yours. So I teach my brain to see the future."

Saeed (Gogo's onboarding specialist/numbers VA) logs into the eXp dashboard monthly and downloads the data to maintain this tracker.

## Eliminate → Automate → Delegate (Business Automation Framework)

The sequence for removing low-value work from the owner's plate. Order matters:

1. **Eliminate** — is this task necessary at all? If not, stop doing it before investing in automating or delegating it.
2. **Automate** — if the task must exist, can a tool handle it? (GHL, ManyChat, Zapier, CRM sequences). Automation scales indefinitely; human labor caps.
3. **Delegate** — if it requires a human, delegate it. Only delegate tasks that cannot be eliminated or automated.

> "Eliminate before you automate. Automate before you delegate. Most people get this backwards."

## Three-Tracker System (Katie — Union Home Mortgage)

[[Katie]] (lender at Union Home Mortgage, 12-year Gogo collaborator) teaches a three-tracker system for maintaining clarity and momentum across every area of her business. She runs all three simultaneously.

**Tracker 1 — The Greatness Tracker:**
- A running log of wins, compliments, closed deals, and positive client feedback
- Purpose: combat the negativity bias that makes bad days feel like trends; gives concrete evidence that you are succeeding
- When a bad week hits, you open the Greatness Tracker and remember what's actually true
- Format: simple running document or spreadsheet; add to it every time something good happens, no matter how small
- "You can't be grateful and negative at the same time"

**Tracker 2 — The Leak Tracker:**
- A log of every task, distraction, or activity that costs you time but isn't money-producing
- Purpose: identify where your hours are actually going; the red-light/green-light audit applied over time
- Every week: look at the leaks, ask "can this be eliminated, automated, or delegated?"
- The leak tracker feeds your VA and delegation decisions — it makes the invisible visible

**Tracker 3 — The Money Tracker:**
- A running record of every dollar earned, every commission, every referral fee, every income stream
- Track by week, month, and quarter
- Purpose: know exactly where your income comes from and identify your highest-ROI activities
- Cross-reference with your time: which activities produced revenue, and at what effective hourly rate?

**Why all three together:**
- Greatness Tracker keeps you motivated
- Leak Tracker keeps you efficient
- Money Tracker keeps you strategic
- Together they create the data to make decisions — not feelings

## VA Hiring: D×E=V Formula (Maria Peskam)

## The Enemy of Good Is Great

One of Gogo's sharpest opportunity cost frameworks: **the enemy of good is not bad — it's great.**

When you've built your skillset to where you can make $2,000/hr on listing work, spending time on a $45/hr task is a losing trade — even if the $45/hr task is "good" by any objective standard. It's good for someone who doesn't have a better option. You do.

Applied to team structure: buyer agents at a 50/50 split earn ~$200/hr — an excellent return for someone without a better option. For the listing-focused top producer, that same work earns $200/hr instead of $2,000/hr. The math is the reason to build a buyer's agent team.

> "The enemy of good is great. You created great opportunities for yourself, but every time you're choosing a good opportunity, you're losing money."

> "When you spend your time on good opportunities, you're stealing time from the great opportunities because you're not going to have time for them."

**The filter question:** At your current level, what is your great opportunity? Every hour spent on a "good" task is a bill you pay from the great opportunity column.

## Two Assistants = 120 Hours Per Week

The math case for hiring two cross-trained assistants rather than one:

- Your 40 hours + Assistant A's 40 hours + Assistant B's 40 hours = **120 hours of work completed per week**
- Nobody's drowning. Nobody's overworked. Everything gets done.
- Side effect: you sleep like a baby.
- When one quits (and eventually one will): life goes on. Cross-training is the insurance policy.

> "If you take your 40 hours and two people's 40 hours, now we are talking 120 hours every week work done. Plus, you sleep like a baby."

Reframe the objection "I can't believe I'm paying two assistants" with: *where will I be at the end of this year with 120 hours of work done every week?* The compounding effect over 12 months is the difference between stuck and scaled.

**Rule:** All of Gogo's help is cross-trained from day one. Single-point-of-failure VAs are a liability.

## Delegation Economics — $200/hr vs. $10 Tasks (Juan Carlos Baronetti)

[[Juan Carlos Baronetti]] of [[Goldbar Staffing]], guesting in the GoGet'Em "Fundamentals" session, sharpened Gogo's hourly-rate math into a staffing case for an executive assistant.

**The core trade.** Agents are worth $200+/hour (Gogo frames her own skillset at $50–$200/hr and up) but spend that time on $10/hr tasks — email, CRM entry, MLS, design, scheduling. Every hour on a low-value task is $190 left on the table.

> "You're all worth $200 an hour or more... and you're trading that $200 an hour for 10 bucks. It's because you are obsessed with doing low value tasks." — [[Juan Carlos Baronetti]]

**The method — audit, then offload.**
1. Audit a full 24 hours of your schedule (the [[#The Red Light / Green Light Exercise|red-light/green-light]] audit applied to time).
2. Identify every $10/hr task on the list.
3. Hand them to an executive assistant.
4. Result: reclaim ~**30 hrs/week** — then reinvest that time into the single highest-ROI activity.

**Content is the highest-ROI protected activity.** The reclaimed hours go straight into content creation — the one-to-many "machine" that compounds. Baronetti cites [[Alex Hormozi]]: he runs $200M/yr companies yet still spends **25 hrs/week** personally on content. If the reclaimed time isn't protected for content (25+ hrs/wk), the delegation loses its leverage.

**Staffing economics (GGTC "Done With You" framing).** One dedicated VA runs ~$12,000/yr; the "Done With You" upgrade rents access to Gogo's 14 assistants twice weekly for 52 weeks at $400/mo ($4,800/yr) — the same delegation logic packaged as shared leverage. Ties directly to the [[#Hourly Rate Formula|hourly-rate filter]] and the [[#The Leverage Hierarchy|leverage hierarchy]] above.

## VA Hiring Mechanics (Jason Matthews — "Speed to Lead")

*Source: [[Speed to Lead - Building a Follow-Up System That Converts (Jason Matthews)]].*

- **ROI, not cost.** Don't ask "what does a VA cost" ($800–$1,500/mo) — ask "what will it make." A VA producing 2 deals/mo at $6K = $12K (10× cost); 2 leads/week ≈ 8 deals/mo ≈ $48K. *"Give me $1,200 and I'll give you back $48,000."*
- **"Buy Back Your Time" ([[Wealth Building|Dan Martell]]).** Jason built a Claude-powered assessment that computes hourly rate, profit margin, and the *silent cost* of self-doing admin. Live example — Melissa: $140K gross, ~$20K net (14% margin), 54 hrs/wk, ~20 hrs on ~$7/hr admin → **~$70K/yr** silently lost.
- **Automate before headcount.** Cheap AI/tools first ($20–29/mo — [[ManyChat]], GoHighLevel), *then* hire.
- **Hire at 75% capacity**, not when your head is already spinning.
- **Always keep two cross-trained VAs** so illness/turnover never breaks operations; a VA is YOUR full-time employee (even via an agency), never a rotating rented worker.
- **SOP-first, ~90-day ramp.** Record each task once into a Google Drive SOP library ("I do it/you watch → you do it/I watch → you do it alone"); budget up to 90 days to fully train.

## Delegate Everything — The 46-Step Insight (Knolly Williams)

*Source: [[The 5 Big Rocks to More Listings and Less Work (Knolly Williams)]].*

[[Knolly Williams]] ran 100+ closed deals/year — a solo peak of **153 transactions in one year with no team production** — and never worked Friday through Sunday, by delegating every task that doesn't legally require his license.

**The 46-step audit.** Knolly's listing business is a **46-step process, and only 3–5 of those steps actually require a real estate license.** Everything else — including inputting the listing into the MLS — does *not* require a license and can be handed off. He does only the license-required steps and delegates the other ~41.

**Mantra: "Delegate everything — you can."** He writes the phrase and then literally crosses out "you can." Own the business, not the job. The belief that keeps agents stuck is *"I'm the only one who can do this."*

> "Delegate everything you *can* — cross out 'you can.' Delegate everything."

The false belief to kill is that the owner is irreplaceable at the task level. Knolly's counter: whatever you're convinced only you can do, someone (or something) else can do it faster — "Claude just did it in 1.3 seconds." Uniqueness lives in the license-required steps and the relationships, not the admin.

**"Do the Two" time-block.** The one non-negotiable, non-delegable money block is a protected **daily 2-hour "lead-generation bunker"** (e.g., 9–11am, undisturbed — no email, no admin, no interruptions). Lead generation is the single green-light activity the owner must personally guard; everything outside those two hours is a candidate for delegation. This maps directly onto the [[#Hourly Rate Formula|hourly-rate filter]] and the [[#The Red Light / Green Light Exercise|red-light/green-light]] audit above — "Do the Two" is the green light; the 41 non-license steps are the red light.

## Circle Coaching — Systems & Delegation

Delegation, calendar, and financial-hygiene tactics Gogo teaches inside her private [[The Circle|Circle]] coaching. NDA-protected; only her generalizable teachings are captured.

### Train Once, Delegate Forever

The reason to push through an unpleasant task instead of avoiding it: you learn it *once*, document it, and hand it off permanently. This is the operating principle behind the whole delegation stack — the discomfort is a one-time cost that buys forever-leverage. Reinforces "hire your weaknesses from day one" — know what you're bad at and offload it immediately, "or you'll hate your job." (Gogo hasn't logged into the transaction platform in 8 years — her #1 reason for low stress.)

> "It's really only hard the very first time until you get it down to a science."

### CEO of a Self-Running Business — "90 Days in Italy"

The target state: a business that runs and earns without you. The stress test Gogo uses — **could you go 90 days in Italy and come back having made the same or more?** If the answer is no, you own a job, not a business. Every train-once-delegate-forever handoff is a step toward passing that test.

### Ritualize Dreaded Work

For the back-office work you keep avoiding (card migration, financial audits, reconciliation), don't try to power through it all at once. **Do it in a low-stakes window, do exactly two items, then stop before you get annoyed — and build in a small reward.** Reframe the work itself as the privilege of success: these are "problems many people never get to have." "Organized chaos" is fine if it works and you know where things are — don't let imperfect order stop you from shipping.

### Card Separation & Automate Bookkeeping by Design

Financial hygiene starts at the card level:

- **Strict card separation** — one business card (in the business's name) for business only, one personal card for personal only. Never cross them. Under audit, a personal card with *zero* personal charges is itself a red flag. "You do not put your child's pink bike on there. Ever."
- **Automate bookkeeping by design** — connect the single business card directly to bookkeeping software so every charge is already categorized. Done right, "there's no bookkeeping" — it's a byproduct of the card setup, not a separate chore. Avoid consumer P2P apps for business spend; they reconcile badly.
- **Bank where personal and business live under one login** for frictionless transfers.

### The Card-Migration Slog

Moving your recurring charges onto the correct separated cards is a real project, not a five-minute task. Method: **print last month's statement and move each recurring payment onto the right card one at a time.** Budget roughly **2 months** to catch everything — and specifically watch for **annual and quarterly charges** that won't appear on any single monthly statement. Delegate it to someone with card and books access. (A dedicated window + "do two, then stop" — see *Ritualize Dreaded Work* above — is how you get through it.)

### On/Off Calendar Rhythm

Don't strand a lone one-hour meeting in the middle of a week — a single isolated block forces a whole day to orbit it. **Cluster meetings so the people you lead get "one day on, one day off."** Consolidate multi-week onboarding (e.g., a 90-day ramp) into fewer, denser days rather than sprinkling short sessions across the calendar. This protects both your maker-time and theirs, and it maps onto Gogo's own compressed Mon–Wed schedule (see [[#Gogo's Current Work Schedule|above]]).

### Two-Phone Discipline + Curate-Before-Delegate Media

- **Two-phone discipline** — carry a dedicated *business* phone whose media auto-uploads to a shared cloud folder your assistants can reach. Keep personal content off it entirely so nothing private is ever in the delegation pipeline.
- **Curate before delegating media** — don't hand editors your entire camera roll. Shoot broadly, then "heart"/select only the approved media into a **shared album**. iPhone-to-iPhone shared albums beat cloud drives on speed and resolution. Editors only ever see what you deliberately curated in.

### Shared-Drive Default for AI Output

When a team runs a single shared AI account, set a **shared drive folder as the default save location** so the whole team gets automatic access to every output — no per-file access requests, no bottleneck. Have your assistants join the setup call so it's implemented once and correctly. (Same logic as the two-phone auto-upload: build the sharing into the pipe, don't do it by hand each time.)

### Subtract Before You Add

Before adding any new activity, product, or channel, **decide what you'll stop doing to make room for it.** Growth layered on top of an already-full plate isn't growth — "growth without it is just more chaos." This is the operational partner to the "2× is harder than 10×" mindset (see [[Mindset]]): real leverage requires removing your own low-value work first, not just piling on. Related discipline: **don't run multiple products at once** — focus on one.
