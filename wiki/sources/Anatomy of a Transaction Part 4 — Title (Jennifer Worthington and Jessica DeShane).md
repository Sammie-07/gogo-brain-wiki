# Anatomy of a Transaction, Part 4 — Title: The Spine of the Transaction (Jennifer Worthington & Jessica DeShane)

> The final installment of the four-part "Anatomy of a Transaction" series on a [[GoGet'Em Community]] / #teamgogo weekly call, explaining how the title company acts as the coordinating "spine" of a real estate transaction and what agents must do to partner with title for smooth closings.

## Key Takeaways
- The four-part "Anatomy of a Transaction" series maps roles to body parts: clients/agents = the heart, transaction coordinators = the nervous system, lenders = the lifeblood, and title = the spine.
- Title is one of only two parties (with the agent) that communicates with everyone — buyers, sellers, both agents, lenders — plus municipalities, taxing authorities, title plants, HOAs, insurers, other lien holders, and other title companies (notably in split-title states like Michigan). Title coordinates but does not control timing.
- Title's core job is to account for every penny and balance funds across the lender, both title companies, buyers, sellers, agents, and brokers; prelim numbers require coordinating 3–5 people plus a quality-control sign-off before release to an agent.
- Jennifer's "5 things to work with your title company": (1) be an agent in flow / stay calm, (2) seek understanding not demands and loop in your transaction coordinator, (3) recognize title is a partner not a commodity or employee, (4) invest in the relationship — "relationships cover a multitude of sins," (5) respect timelines and the risk title carries.
- Jessica's "5 things title needs from agents": (1) complete & accurate contract info, (2) early notice of special situations (trust, probate, estate, divorce/separation, power of attorney), (3) HOA information up front, (4) clear communication on closing changes, (5) identity & documentation.
- Jessica's "5 things agents misunderstand about title": (1) title finds problems, doesn't create them — early bad news is protection not delay; (2) title is neutral, a third party to the contract/lender/underwriter/state & federal law; (3) underwriters set the rules, not title companies; (4) title can't fix anything without documentation; (5) communication and timing matter more than agents realize.
- Entity/ownership verification: listing agents should involve title BEFORE the listing agreement to confirm ownership; individuals vs. LLCs vs. trusts must be correct or the purchase agreement may be invalid. LLCs require an operating agreement (identifies who can legally sign) plus a certificate of good standing (title can pull from the state); trusts require a certificate of trust. If no operating agreement exists, the sole owner must confirm in writing.
- Fraud/identity: Clear Title is adopting a third-party tool, Certified ID (selfie + ID verification), to identify parties earlier than the closing table, because title insurance underwriters increasingly require stronger identity guarantees and a driver's license at closing is becoming insufficient. Valid (non-expired) government ID or passport is required.
- Closing logistics: lenders increasingly allow RON (Remote Online Notary) closings via Zoom-like sessions; closings can be mobile almost anywhere — cited examples include a cruise ship on the Danube, Portugal, Uganda, a U.S. embassy in Mexico, Disneyland, coffee shops, restaurants, and homes. Clear Title contracts professional, insured closers versed in mortgage documents.
- Lien-search / title-pull timing: on the sell side, pull title when the listing is taken; on the buy side, order after inspections clear. Pulling title costs ~$200 per pull in most Michigan counties. Turnaround is ~48 hours for title back + ~24 hours for exam (~3–4 days total); rural / Upper Peninsula counties requiring manual courthouse searches take longer.
- Agent response-rate gap: only ~25% of agents respond to the standard post-title-request email. Example — an agent never confirmed their admin/compliance fee, so it was omitted from the prelim CD; the lender refused to add it until Jessica produced the buyer's exclusive agreement to prove disclosure.
