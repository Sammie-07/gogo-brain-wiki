## Key Teachings (de-identified)

### Tax strategy (folds into [[Tax Strategy]])
- **Meet your CPA quarterly, not annually.** Once you earn real money, quarterly meetings are mandatory. A first tax conversation in Nov/Dec is too late — every lever (max retirement accounts, hire a family member, buy a vehicle/property) is gone once the year closes.
- **Buy real estate to reduce taxable income, not for monthly cash flow.** The point isn't the small monthly profit — it's shrinking your tax bill while a tenant pays down the mortgage and the asset appreciates.
- **Cost segregation on every rental.** A separate engineering study (a few thousand dollars each) your CPA does *not* do by default — you commission it and hand the report to the CPA. Roughly ~30% of a property's value can be written off. Do only as many per year as needed to offset that year's income. Red flag: a CPA who's never mentioned it probably doesn't know it. Doesn't require an LLC.
- **Weigh help against tax saved.** Property management (~10%) and bookkeeping feel like they erode thin margins, but against tax savings they're trivial.
- **1031 exchange discipline.** A like-kind exchange defers the gain on an appreciated rental. Line up a qualified intermediary in advance; ~90-day reinvest window; proceeds go **title-company to title-company** — the money can never touch your personal account, even for a second, or it's taxable.
- **Buy-and-hold.** Selling creates taxable income; the general plan is to buy and hold inside the trust and let assets compound.

### Asset protection — "box inside a box inside a box" (folds into [[Legal Protection]])
- **One LLC per property**, each with its own bank account and card. Never pool multiple rentals or run them through one account — a lawsuit over one property exposes the whole shared account.
- **Structure:** property → its own **LLC** → monthly profit sweeps **up to a holding company** (never leave more than bill-paying money in the property account) → holding company owned by a **living trust** (not public record) → children named as **trustees, not owners** (they can manage/draw salaries but don't "own" it, avoiding inheritance tax).
- **Registered agent** on each entity to shield identity in public records — so no one can total up what you own or target other assets after a first lawsuit.
- **Avoid "piercing the corporate veil":** co-mingling personal and business money is the #1 way to void LLC protection. Each entity's card is used only for that entity.
- **Confirm before moving a mortgaged property into an LLC** — some lenders treat the transfer as an ownership/commercial change and could call the loan due; use experienced practitioner-advisors.
- **Roll out without overwhelm:** one property per month (LLC → bank account → next). An unlimited-LLC service with a single dashboard/"blueprint" of all entities pays for itself at scale.

### Financial hygiene (folds into [[Productivity]] + [[Wealth Building]])
- **Strict card separation** — one business card (in the LLC name) for business, one personal card for personal; never cross them. Under audit, a personal card with zero personal charges is a flag. "You do not put your child's pink bike on there. Ever."
- **Bank where personal + business live under one login** for easy transfers.
- **Automate bookkeeping by design** — connect the single business card to bookkeeping software so it's already categorized ("there's no bookkeeping"). Don't use consumer P2P apps for business; they reconcile badly.
- **The card-migration slog:** print last month's statement and move each recurring payment onto the correct card one by one; budget ~2 months; watch for annual/quarterly charges; delegate to someone with card + books access.

### Delegation & mindset
- **Train once, delegate forever** — the reason to push through an unpleasant task and hand it off. Goal: become CEO of a self-running business — "go 90 days in Italy and come back having made the same or more."
- **"Organized chaos" is fine** if it works and you know where things are — don't let imperfect order stop you.
- **Reframe hard back-office work as the privilege of success** — "problems many people never get to have." Ritualize dreaded work: do it in a low-stakes window, do two items, then stop before you get annoyed, and build a small reward.
