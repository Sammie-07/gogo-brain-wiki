# People & Team Building, Ep. 149 (John Kitchens)

> John Kitchens' Episode 149 on building and managing a high-performance team. Covers the Functional Accountability Chart, the agreements vs. expectations distinction, the enthusiastic rehire test, ones-and-threes performance scoring, and the principle that compensation structure drives behavior.

## Key Takeaways

- **FAC — Functional Accountability Chart (from Verne Harnish, *Scaling Up*):** Every seat on the team has a clear function and a single accountable owner. Not a hierarchy chart — a function map. Each box answers: "What does this seat exist to accomplish?" If a function has no owner, it will not get done.
- **Agreements vs. expectations:** Expectations are what you assume someone will do. Agreements are explicit, mutual commitments with a specific deliverable and deadline. Team conflict almost always stems from unspoken expectations masquerading as agreements. Write down agreements; never leave expectations implied.
- **"Would you enthusiastically rehire this person?":** The hiring filter applied retroactively. If you pause before answering — or the answer is "no" — that's the data. Teams underperform when they carry members no one would enthusiastically rehire. Removing underperformers is not punitive; it is the leader's primary fiduciary duty to the team.
- **Ones and Threes concept:** Team members are scored as:
  - **1** = performing at or above expectations — keep, invest in, develop
  - **3** = underperforming — on a performance plan, limited timeline to turn around
  There is no 2. No one is "kind of fine." If you can't clearly call them a 1, they're a 3. This forces honest assessment and prevents the "he's doing okay" drift that lets underperformers coast.
- **Compensation drives behavior:** Your compensation structure is your behavior design. Commission-only agents optimize for their own production. Revenue share participants think about recruitment. Salary + bonus agents optimize for KPIs in the bonus formula. Before changing a team member's behavior, examine whether the comp structure rewards the behavior you want.

## Quotes

> "If your answer to 'would you enthusiastically rehire this person?' isn't an immediate yes — that's your answer."

> "Expectations are what you assume. Agreements are what you committed to. Most team problems are just unspoken expectations dressed up as agreements."

> "There is no 2. If you can't call someone a 1, they're a 3. That's the whole system."
